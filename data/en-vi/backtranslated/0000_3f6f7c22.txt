ABSTRACT
Currently, competency-based assessment is an emerging trend in the global education sector.
Accordingly, developing and standardizing competency assessment tools is receiving special attention from educators.
For STEM education, a standardized competency assessment tool that is aligned with practical contexts plays an important role in guiding future development.
This study aims to develop and standardize a tool for assessing STEM competencies of high school students in Ho Chi Minh City as students engage in STEM-oriented learning.
To ensure the tool’s reliability and validity, the research process was rigorously implemented in three stages: question design, scale development, and scale evaluation.
Quantitative analysis results show that the tool is reliable, valid, and appropriate for assessing STEM competencies of local students.
Therefore, this set of tools can serve as a means for students to self-assess their competencies or as a competency framework to support teachers in developing other assessment instruments during STEM-oriented learning.
Keywords: CB-SEM; competency assessment; high school students; STEM competency
1.
Introduction
In recent years, along with the transition to a competency-based approach in educational curricula, assessment in education has generally shifted from evaluating learners' ability to reproduce knowledge to assessing their competencies (Shulman, 2008).
Competency assessment not only provides objective remarks and conclusions about students’ learning and training processes and outcomes but also focuses on identifying strengths and limitations within students’ competency structures.
This is significant as it offers both qualitative and quantitative feedback on the effectiveness of students’ learning processes and teachers’ instruction.
As a result, students can determine appropriate adjustments to enhance their own learning quality, while teachers can devise measures to help students continuously improve, developing the necessary competencies and qualities to prepare for future endeavors.

The Resolution of the Eighth Plenum of the Party Central Committee (11th tenure) clearly states: “Examinations, testing, and evaluation of educational outcomes need to be gradually implemented in accordance with advanced criteria that are trusted and recognized by society and the global educational community.
It is essential to combine the use of assessment results during the learning process with end-of-term and end-of-year evaluations; combine teachers’ assessments with students’ self-assessment; and combine school evaluations with assessments from families and society” (General Secretary, 2013).
Thus, it can be affirmed that assessment activities or competency assessment are not solely the responsibility of teachers, but also require the integration of self-assessment and self-monitoring of competency development by the learners themselves.
For STEM education, the role of competency assessment for students is further emphasized as assessment tools for measuring student STEM competency are considered essential for enhancing the quality of STEM education, given their feedback on the effectiveness of each learning stage (Arikan, Erktin, & Pesen, 2020).
However, accurately assessing human competency in general, and student competency in particular, is not easy; it requires a rigorous research process to ensure both reliability and validity—factors that reflect the quality of measurement scales (Baartman et al., 2006).
Therefore, educational researchers worldwide are continuously striving to combine qualitative and quantitative research methods in the development of competency assessment tools.
In Vietnam, most competency assessment tools for students studying under a STEM-oriented approach are built based on theoretical frameworks inherited from global research, without standardization or adjustments to fit the characteristics and degree of STEM education implementation in actual practice using empirical data.
This leads to difficulties in synchronously implementing assessments or selecting appropriate tools to evaluate students’ results and learning processes in STEM-oriented education.
Thus, developing and standardizing a STEM competency assessment tool that ensures reliability, validity, and suitability for the level of STEM education implementation in each locality in Vietnam is necessary.
For this reason, we have carried out the development and standardization of a STEM competency assessment tool for high school students in Ho Chi Minh City.
Through literature review, in-depth interviews with experts, and statistical analysis of data collected from 544 high school students who participated in locally conducted STEM lessons, the research results yielded a STEM competency assessment tool for students that meets the standards of reliability, validity, and overall suitability.
2.
Subjects and research methods 2.1.
Assessing students' competencies with a standardized assessment tool 2.1.1.
Competency-based student assessment Assessing learners’ competencies is a crucial process for guiding the development of students’ competencies.
In this context, competency is understood as a personal attribute that is formed and developed based on innate qualities and the process of learning and training, enabling individuals to effectively mobilize an integrated set of knowledge, skills, and other personal attributes such as interests, beliefs, willpower, etc., to successfully perform a specific type of activity and achieve desired results under certain conditions (MOET, 2018).
Structurally, competency can be modeled as a system composed of components including composite competencies, constituent competencies, and behavioral indicators, which are manifested through the learning process (Bình, 2015).
The composite component of a competency pertains to the professional domain that shapes the competency.
From there, constituent competencies are distilled from the composite competencies, each carrying distinct characteristics from their respective professional domains.
Both of these components are latent factors that cannot be directly assessed; instead, they must be inferred through the expression of corresponding sets of behavioral indicators over a certain period of time.
Behavioral indicators are data that provide information about the competency, serving the purpose of assessment throughout the learning process.
Behaviors manifested externally through activities such as being able to write, express opinions, create, etc., form the basis for measurement to determine the extent to which a competency has been achieved.
Thus, the essence of assessing students’ competencies lies in the process of checking and measuring the degree to which competencies are attained when students successfully perform tasks and solve problems during learning, through the collection and analysis of students' externally displayed behavioral manifestations (Baartman et al., 2006).

Teachers can use the framework to design checklists, rubrics, and tests; to assess competencies; and to develop appropriate lesson plans for fostering and developing students’ abilities.

2.1.2.
Criteria for the reliability and validity of standardized competency assessment tools
According to the positivist school, validity and reliability are two fundamental cognitive tools and basic standards for standardizing an assessment tool, such as a STEM competency assessment tool (Winter, 2000).
In particular, the validity of a measurement tool refers to how accurately the tool measures the intended object.
Specifically, for an assessment tool to be valid, it must satisfy the following criteria: content validity, measurement validity, and construct validity.
Content validity reflects the extent to which the tool accurately expresses the intended content.
Thus, a measurement tool needs to be standardized in terms of content by expressing its meaning clearly, focusing on the core, and being succinct; to achieve effective content refinement, the tool requires expert consultation on its presentation, typically using the Delphi research method.
Measurement validity is determined through convergent validity and discriminant validity.
Convergent validity indicates the degree of correlation between measurement variables (behavior indicators) within the same component.
Discriminant validity reflects the distinction between competencies represented by different components within the same competency structure.
In quantitative research on competency measurement tools, convergent validity is defined by the level of correlation among behavior indicators, while discriminant validity is examined by determining whether components within the competency are truly distinct from one another.
Construct validity relates to the extent to which the tool measures the concepts, behaviors, ideas, and qualities of the theoretical structure.
In competency assessment, by empirically testing construct validity, researchers can model the theoretical structure—accept, modify, or propose a new structure that fits the quantitative results.
On the other hand, to present the concept of reliability, Joppe (2000) described it as follows: “The extent to which results do not change over time and represent an accurate depiction of the population under study is called reliability.
If the results of a study can be replicated under a similar methodology, then the measurement tool is considered reliable.” Thus, the reliability of a competency assessment tool can be understood as the stability of that tool.
The reliability of a scale must satisfy the following criteria: stability, equivalence, and internal consistency.
The stability of a competency assessment tool is reflected in the requirement that it must yield stable results over time across equivalent samples.
That is, if a scale is tested on groups of samples with similar characteristics over an appropriate period, it should produce similar results.
Specifically, when evaluating the stability of a competency assessment tool, a pilot survey with a small sample and a large-scale survey should be conducted to eliminate inappropriate variables.
The equivalence of a measurement tool is evaluated by expert groups.
If all members of the expert group share the same opinion about the tool, then the tool is considered equivalent.
The equivalence of the competency assessment tool is examined through expert consultation; variables with high consensus are retained, while those with divergent opinions are reviewed for potential adjustment.
Internal consistency requires that groups within the sample, when measured by the tool, should yield consistent results.
The sample is divided into smaller groups that fully represent the overall data; measurements are conducted on each group, and the results should align.
If discrepancies are found, adjustments to the tool should be considered.

STEM Competency of Students 2.2.1.

STEM education is an educational model based on an interdisciplinary approach, enabling students to apply knowledge from science, technology, engineering, and mathematics to solve practical problems in specific contexts (MOET, 2018).
To evaluate the impacts of this educational model on students, Boon Ng (2019) studied the STEM competency of students within the framework of education based on both STEM subjects and subjects unrelated to STEM (such as language, culture, literature, etc.).
The research results show that when studying STEM subjects, students exhibit behavioral characteristics specific to the fields of science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (Boon Ng, 2019).
During the learning process, students approach learning tasks in ways similar to how scientists, mathematicians, and engineers solve problems and conduct research.
Students actively explore knowledge through inquiry-based activities, analysis, and synthesis, utilizing knowledge to serve learning purposes.
When participating in STEM lessons, students investigate and identify issues relevant to real life.
Drawing on their accumulated knowledge and skills, students propose and select optimal solutions, perform technical operations to create products or models.
Throughout the learning process, students engage in group discussions and exchanges to present their own ideas, contribute to lesson development, and defend the group’s viewpoint through argumentation.
Students share the process, results, and products of their projects with others.
Based on the analysis results, within the scope of this article, students' STEM competency is understood as the ability to integrate and mobilize knowledge and skills in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics to solve practical problems in specific contexts, thereby creating value for both individuals and the community.
2.2.2.
Theoretical model of students’ STEM competency
The structure of students’ STEM competency is constructed based on the analysis and synthesis of recurring competency components found in 13 international studies on STEM competency framework structures.
The STEM competency structure consists of 5 components:
Component 1.
Information collection
The competency of information collection is understood as the ability to identify the information to be sought, find sources of information, and record information according to initial needs and purposes.
Bundy’s (2004) research points out that this competency helps learners recognize the need to seek out information, determine the required level of information, and access information efficiently.
According to the SCONUL information competency model by Bainton (2001), this competency is manifested in behaviors such as: recognizing the need to collect information, seeking sources of information, developing information search strategies, locating and accessing information.
The information collection competency can be observed through behaviors such as: listening and answering questions, observation, searching for sources of information, investigating and exploring, collecting information (planning and recording data), and making conclusions/decisions (Boon Ng, 2019).
Component 2.
Processing and utilizing information
The group of behaviors related to the processing and utilization of information in STEM competency encompasses an integration of actions such as synthesizing data, comparing data, classifying data, managing and storing data, evaluating, and analyzing data (Boon Ng, 2019).
The SCONUL model identifies information processing and utilization competency as being demonstrated by learners through activities such as comparing, evaluating information, organizing, and synthesizing information (Bainton, 2001).
Bundy (2004) identified behaviors corresponding to the group of information processing behaviors, including: evaluating information sources, classifying, storing information, and using information to solve problems.
Component 3.
Solution Implementation Competency
The solution implementation competency involves a set of behavioral indicators representative of problem-solving ability.
Students actualize solutions, design, and create products to address issues (Watkins, Spencer, & Hammer, 2014).
English (2016) identifies several behavioral indicators related to solution implementation competency, including planning, preparing materials, designing drawings, assessing feasibility, and manufacturing products.
Additionally, research by Sen et al.
(2018) demonstrates that solution implementation competency is manifested through behaviors such as identifying causes and deficiencies in products, making improvements, and orienting development in new contexts.
Component 4.
Technical Safety
Technical safety competency is a component competency that demonstrates students’ skills in carrying out technical operations while adhering to safety rules during the process of making products.
This competency helps students understand the role and usage of technical tools and equipment; it also emphasizes adherence to technical safety regulations to protect themselves and others from unfortunate accidents.
Based on the research of Boon Ng (2019), the indicators of the technical practice behavior group include: using equipment (identifying its functions and using it correctly), maintaining equipment (preserving and ensuring safety), and calibrating devices and instruments when necessary.
Component 5.
Community Sharing Competency
The community sharing competency enables students to empathize with those facing difficulties, motivating them to share their learning processes, outcomes, and products with the community.
In the STEM education framework of The New York Academy of Sciences, it is required to provide opportunities for students to demonstrate their ability to communicate clearly, accurately, and persuasively, as well as to utilize a variety of methods to convey ideas.
Additionally, it is necessary to create an environment where students can discuss and critically evaluate STEM arguments (GSA, 2016).
Trilling (2009) proposed community sharing behaviors: presenting ideas in specified forms (reports, written texts, posters, videos, etc.
), listening to feedback and developing ideas, and identifying the purpose of sharing.
2.3.
Research Methodology
The study applies the process by Boateng et al.
(2018) with three stages: item design, scale development, and scale evaluation.
2.3.1.
Question Design Phase
In the question design phase, based on theoretical research, the STEM competency assessment tool for students comprises five components: Information Collection (TT), Information Processing and Utilization (XS), Solution Implementation (TH), Technical Safety (SD), and Information Sharing (CS).
Accordingly, the initial questionnaire consists of 15 Likert-type behavioral indicators, with each item including a behavioral description and a five-level response option.
The behavioral indicators are coded sequentially from A01 to A15.
The Delphi technique was conducted with 21 STEM education experts, including 4 university lecturers of pedagogy and 17 STEM teachers with at least 2 years of experience in teaching STEM at high schools, to refine the content validity of each behavioral description for learners.
Descriptions assessed as lacking adequate content validity were either removed or replaced, and unclear items were revised.
The language was adjusted to ensure suitability for high school students.
The scale resulting from the qualitative study was then used to design the survey questionnaire.
2.3.2.
Scale development stage
Next, quantitative studies on a small sample size will be conducted to preliminarily assess the scales of the study's constructs.
The evaluation criteria for the scales at this stage include reliability and validity.
Reliability is assessed through the criterion of Cronbach’s Alpha coefficient being above 0.7 and item-total correlation coefficient greater than 0.3 (Nunnally & Bernstein, 1994).
Convergent validity of the scale is evaluated through the factor loading (FL) coefficient in exploratory factor analysis (EFA), with the minimum acceptable factor loading being 0.5; if the cumulative variance percentage exceeds 50%, the scale is considered to have convergent validity (Gerbing & Anderson, 1988).
In addition, for each observed variable, the difference between the highest factor loading and the other factor loadings (in absolute value) must reach 0.3 or higher to ensure discriminant validity (Bryant & Yarnold, 1995).
Preliminary analyses at this stage help reduce the number of behavioral indicators in the competency assessment tool as well as extract the number of components in the STEM competency structure of students.
2.3.3.
Scale evaluation stage
Finally, the main study is conducted on a large sample size.
The results obtained will undergo evaluation of the remaining aspects of reliability and validity, as well as an assessment of the model's structural fit to the population using AMOS 24 software.
 Assessment of reliability, convergent validity, and discriminant validity
Internal consistency reliability of the scale is determined based on two indices: Cronbach’s alpha coefficient and composite reliability (CR) (Devellis & Thorpe, 2021).
The higher the Cronbach’s alpha coefficient, the greater the internal consistency reliability.
Composite reliability ranges from 0 to 1 and is considered good when its value exceeds 0.7.
Convergent validity indicates the extent to which the indicators for a construct share a common variance, that is, to achieve convergent validity, the set of variables measuring a research construct must be highly correlated (Kline, 2016).
This is reflected in the standardized regression coefficients for each variable of the latent construct when it is a unidimensional scale.
For a scale to achieve convergent validity, the standardized loading of each component variable must be greater than 0.5 and statistically significant (Gerbing & Anderson, 1988).
Additionally, convergent validity is also assessed through the average variance extracted (AVE).
An AVE coefficient greater than or equal to 0.5 confirms convergent validity (Hair et al., 2019).
Discriminant validity indicates the extent to which a construct is truly distinct statistically from another construct in terms of both correlation and measurement variables within each concept (Hair et al., 2019).
The traditional approach to assessing discriminant validity is the Fornell-Larcker criterion, which is established by the condition that the square root of the AVE must be greater than the inter-construct correlations.
Moreover, another assessment criterion is that the correlation between two constructs must be less than 0.85, and the maximum shared variance (MSV) must be less than the AVE (Kline, 2016).
 Testing the Fit of the Students’ Competency Structural Model
First, the chi-square/degrees of freedom ratio (χ²/df) is an index used to assess the fit of the measurement model, with values less than 5 considered acceptable and values less than 3 considered good (Hair et al., 2019).
In addition, other commonly used indices according to Kline (2016) are also important, including: absolute fit index – GFI (goodness-of-fit index); adjusted goodness-of-fit index according to degrees of freedom – AGFI (adjusted goodness-of-fit statistics); relative fit index – CFI (comparative fit index); Tucker and Lewis index – TLI (Tucker-Lewis index); and the root mean square error of approximation – RMSEA, which assesses model fit compared to the population.
The GFI, AGFI, TLI, and CFI indices are considered to indicate a good fit to actual data if they are greater than 0.9 (Hair et al., 2019).
A maximum RMSEA value of 0.08 indicates a good model fit, and an RMSEA less than 0.03 indicates a very good fit.

The results from the Delphi method enabled the research team to effectively gather expert opinions on the theoretical framework of STEM competency for high school students.
In the first Delphi round, expert opinions were not highly consistent for several indicators—in particular, there was a significant gap (over 10%) in the level of agreement between university lecturers and high school teachers on indicators A02, A03, A08, A09, A10, and A11.
After the Delphi revision rounds, the descriptions of the behavioral indicators in the theoretical STEM competency framework for students were adjusted in terms of content according to the consensus of the Delphi expert panel (Figure 2).

3.2.
Research Sample
Regarding sample size, according to Hair et al.
(2019), for studies utilizing SEM analysis with models containing up to seven research constructs, the minimum sample size required is 300.
This study employed a cumulative random sampling method to collect information from students at nine high schools, including: Luong The Vinh High School (District 1), Le Hong Phong Specialized High School (District 5), High School for Gifted Students – Vietnam National University (District 5), Pham Phu Thu High School (District 6), Nam Sai Gon Secondary and High School (District 7), Nguyen Du High School (District 10), Nguyen Huu Canh High School (Binh Tan District), Gia Dinh High School (Binh Thanh District), and Hoa Sen Secondary and High School (Thu Duc City).
The sampling process took place from December 2021 to early April 2022.
After distributing questionnaires both online and in person to high schools across the city, more than 887 students participated in the survey.
Following a verification process and the elimination of unreliable responses and students who had never participated in STEM-oriented education, a sample of 544 students was selected for analysis.

3.3.
Preliminary Assessment of the Reliability and Validity of the Scale
Before conducting the measurement model test of students' STEM competency using CB-SEM, exploratory factor analysis (EFA) was carried out to reduce or eliminate behavioral indicators that were inappropriate in the competency scale and to determine the number of competency components present in the structure of students' STEM competency.
Preliminary analysis results showed that item A01 of the Information Collection scale and item A06 of the Information Processing and Utilization scale had factor loading differences between factor groups of less than 0.3, indicating that these items did not meet discriminant validity and were therefore excluded.
In addition, the preliminary EFA analysis yielded three competency components instead of the original five.
Observing that students' behavioral indicators still followed the learning process guided by STEM education, we renamed the components as follows: Information Collection and Processing (IN), Solution Implementation and Community Sharing (PS), and Use of Equipment and Technical Tools (TE).
The behavioral indicators were also recoded accordingly: A02, A03, A04, and A05 were replaced by IN01, IN02, IN03, and IN04, respectively; A07, A08, A09, A10, A11, and A12 were replaced by PS01, PS02, PS03, PS04, PS05, and PS06, respectively; and A13, A14, and A15 were replaced by TE01, TE02, and TE03, respectively.

Conclusion
Thus, this study has developed and standardized a competency assessment tool for high school students in the context of STEM-oriented education.
After refinement, the tool comprises three components of STEM competency: Information Collection and Processing (IN), Solution Implementation and Community Sharing (PS), and Use of Equipment and Technical Tools (TE), all measured using a 7-point Likert scale.
The higher the average scores across all behavioral indicators, the greater the students’ capacity to meet the demands of STEM-oriented learning.
Through a rigorous research process consisting of three stages—item design, scale development, and scale evaluation—the CB-SEM analysis results indicate that the constructed tool meets the criteria for internal consistency reliability and validity.

ABSTRACT
Nowadays, competency assessment is one of the global assessment trends.
Accordingly, the development and standardization of competency assessment tools have attracted much attention from educators.
For STEM education, a standardized and practical STEM competency assessment tool plays a vital role in guiding future developments.
This study aims to develop and standardize a tool for self-assessment of STEM competency among students in Ho Chi Minh City.
In order to ensure the reliability and validity of the tool, the research process was strictly conducted in three stages: questionnaire design, scale development, and scale evaluation.
Quantitative analysis results show that the tool is reliable, valid, and appropriate for evaluating the STEM competency of students in the region.
Thus, this tool is suitable for students to self-assess their competencies or as a competency framework to support teachers in developing other assessment tools for STEM learning.
