ABSTRACT

Currently, competency-based assessment is a prevailing trend in education worldwide. Accordingly, the development and standardization of competency assessment instruments have attracted special attention from educators. For STEM education, a standardized, practice-relevant STEM competency assessment tool plays an important role in guiding future development. This study aims to develop and standardize a STEM competency assessment tool for high school students in Ho Chi Minh City during their learning under a STEM education orientation. To ensure the reliability and validity of the instrument, the research process was rigorously conducted in three stages: item design, scale development, and scale evaluation. Quantitative analysis results indicate that the instrument is reliable, valid, and suitable for assessing the STEM competencies of local students. Consequently, this toolkit can serve as a means for students to self-assess their competencies or as a competency framework to support teachers in developing other assessment instruments during students' STEM-oriented learning.

Keywords: CB-SEM; competency assessment; high school students; STEM competencies

1. Introduction
   In recent years, alongside curriculum reform toward a competency-based approach, assessment in education has generally shifted from evaluating the ability to reproduce knowledge to assessing learners' competencies (Shulman, 2008). Competency assessment not only offers objective observations and conclusions about students' learning and training processes and outcomes, but also emphasizes identifying strengths and limitations within students' competency structures. This provides both qualitative and quantitative feedback on the effectiveness of students' learning and teachers' instruction. Through this, students can identify appropriate directions for adjustment to improve their own learning quality, and teachers can find measures to help students continually progress and develop the necessary competencies and qualities to prepare for adapting to global changes, thereby contributing to the achievement of the stated educational objectives.

Coordinate the use of assessment results during the learning process with end-of-term and end-of-year assessments; teacher assessments with learners’ self-assessments; school assessments with assessments by families and society” (General Secretary, 2013). Thus, it can be concluded that evaluation or competency assessment is not an isolated activity of teachers but must be combined with learners’ self-assessment and self-monitoring of their own competency development.

For STEM education, the role of assessing learners’ competencies is further affirmed, since tools for assessing and measuring students’ STEM competencies are considered essential to improve the quality of STEM education by providing feedback on the effectiveness of each stage of learning (Arikan, Erktin, & Pesen, 2020). However, accurately assessing human competence in general, and students' competence in particular, is not easy; a rigorous research process is required to ensure both reliability and validity — the factors that indicate the quality of a measurement scale (Baartman et al., 2006). Therefore, education researchers worldwide have continuously sought to combine qualitative and quantitative research methods in the process of developing competency assessment instruments. In Vietnam, most tools for assessing students' competencies in STEM-oriented education have been constructed based on theoretical frameworks inherited from international studies, without being standardized or adjusted to fit the characteristics and the level of STEM implementation in practice using empirical data. This leads to difficulties when implementing synchronized assessments or when selecting tools to evaluate outcomes and the learning processes of students in STEM-oriented education. Hence, developing and standardizing a STEM competency assessment instrument that ensures reliability and validity and is appropriate for the level of STEM implementation in each locality in Vietnam is necessary. For these reasons, we carried out the development and standardization of a STEM competency assessment instrument for high school students in Ho Chi Minh City (HCMC). Through a literature review, in-depth interviews with a team of experts, and the collection and statistical analysis of data from 544 high school students who had participated in local STEM lessons, the study produced a STEM competency assessment tool for students that meets standards of reliability, validity, and overall suitability.

2. Participants and Research Methods
   2.1. Assessment of students' competencies using a standardized assessment tool
   2.1.1. Assessing students using a competency-based approach
   Assessing learners' competencies is an important process aimed at guiding the development of students' competencies. In this context, competency is understood as a personal attribute formed and developed through innate traits and the process of learning and practice, enabling an individual to mobilize and integrate knowledge, skills, and other personal attributes such as interests, beliefs, and determination to successfully perform a particular type of activity and achieve desired outcomes under specific conditions (MOET, 2018). Structurally, competency can be modeled as a system composed of components including component competencies, constituent competencies, and behavioral indicators manifested through the learning process (Bình, 2015). A component of competency relates to the professional domain that forms the competency. Constituent competencies, extracted from the composite competency, carry characteristic properties from those professional domains. Both of these components are latent factors that cannot be directly assessed but must be inferred through the manifestations of corresponding sets of behavioral indicators over a given period of time. Behavioral indicators are data that provide information about competency and serve assessment purposes throughout the learning process. Behaviors expressed externally through activities—being able to write, to present, to produce, etc.—are the basis for measurement to determine the level of competency attainment. Thus, the essence of assessing students' competencies is the process of testing and measuring the extent to which a competency has been achieved when students successfully carry out tasks and tackle problems during the learning process, through collecting and analyzing students' externally observable behavioral manifestations (Baartman et al., 2006).

2.1.2. Criteria for the reliability and validity of standardized competency assessment instruments

According to the positivist school, validity and reliability are two basic epistemic tools and fundamental criteria for standardizing an assessment instrument, for example an instrument for assessing STEM competencies (Winter, 2000). Validity of a measurement instrument indicates the degree to which the instrument accurately measures what it is intended to measure. Specifically, for an assessment instrument to be valid it must satisfy the following criteria: content validity, measurement validity, and construct validity. Content validity reflects the accuracy of the instrument’s content expression. Thus, an instrument needs to be standardized in terms of content through clear wording, focus, and conciseness; to effectively refine content, the instrument should be presented for consultation to field experts using the Delphi method. Measurement validity is determined through convergent and discriminant validity. Convergent validity indicates the degree of correlation among measurement variables (behavioral indicators) within the same factor. Discriminant validity reflects the distinction between these component competencies and other factors within the same competency structure. In quantitative research on competency measurement instruments, convergent validity is determined by the correlations among behavioral indicators, while discriminant validity is examined by assessing whether the factors within the competency are truly distinct from one another. Construct validity relates to the extent to which the measurement instrument captures the concepts, behaviors, ideas, and qualities of the theoretical structure. In competency assessment, by testing construct validity researchers can model the theoretical structure, accept or adjust it, or propose a new structure that aligns with the quantitative results.

If research results can be replicated using a similar methodology, then the measurement instrument is considered reliable." Thus, the reliability of an assessment instrument can be understood as the degree of stability of that instrument. The reliability of a scale must meet the following criteria: stability; equivalence; internal consistency.

The stability of the competency assessment instrument is reflected in the requirement that the measurement tool maintain consistency over time across equivalent samples. In other words, if a scale is tested on sample groups with the same characteristics within an appropriate time interval, the scale should yield similar results. Specifically, when testing the stability of a competency assessment instrument, a pilot survey with a small sample and a large-scale survey should be conducted to eliminate inappropriate variables.

The equivalence of the measurement instrument is examined by consulting groups of experts. If each member of the expert group shares the same view of the instrument, then the instrument can be considered equivalent. The equivalence of the competency assessment instrument is verified by soliciting expert opinions: variables with high consensus are retained, while variables with divergent views are considered for revision.

The internal consistency of subgroups within the sample, when measured by a single instrument, must produce consistent results. Divide the sample into smaller groups that each contain the full range of sample data, administer the measurement to each subgroup, and the results obtained should be consistent; if inconsistencies arise, adjustments should be considered. STEM education is an interdisciplinary model that helps students apply knowledge from science, technology, engineering, and mathematics to solve practical problems in specific contexts (MOET, 2018). To assess the impacts of this educational model on students, Boon Ng (2019) studied students’ STEM competencies within education based on STEM subjects as well as non-STEM subjects (language, culture, literature, etc.). The study found that when engaging in STEM subjects, students exhibit behavioral manifestations characteristic of the domains of science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (Boon Ng, 2019). In the learning process, students approach learning tasks in the manner of scientists, mathematicians, and engineers when solving problems and conducting inquiries. Students actively explore knowledge through activities of information seeking, analysis, and synthesis, using knowledge for learning purposes. When participating in STEM lessons, students investigate and identify problems related to real-life contexts. Based on the knowledge and skills they have accumulated, students propose and select optimal solutions, carry out technical operations to fabricate products or models. Throughout the learning process, students engage in exchanges and group discussions to present their own ideas, offer constructive input to the lesson, and critically defend their group’s viewpoints. Students share the process, results, and products of their projects with others. From the analysis results, within the scope of this paper, high school students' STEM competency is understood as the ability to mobilize and integrate knowledge and skills in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics to solve real-world problems in specific contexts, thereby creating value for individuals and the community. Theoretical model of students' STEM competencies

The structure of students' STEM competencies was developed based on analysis and synthesis of recurring competency elements in 13 research studies on STEM competency frameworks worldwide. The structure comprises five components:

Component 1. Information gathering
The information-gathering component is understood as the ability to identify what information needs to be sought, find information sources, and record information according to initial needs and purposes. Bundy (2004) indicates that the information-gathering component helps learners recognize their need for information, determine the level of information required, and access information effectively. Based on the SCONUL model of information skills by Bainton (2001), the information-gathering competence is manifested in behaviors such as recognizing the need to collect information, searching for information sources, developing search strategies, and locating and accessing information. The information-gathering component is evidenced by behaviors such as listening and answering questions, observing, searching for information sources, investigating and exploring, collecting information (planning and recording data), and drawing conclusions/decisions (Boon Ng, 2019).

Component 2. Processing and using information
The behavioral expressions for processing and using information in STEM competence encompass behaviors such as synthesizing data, comparing data, classifying data, managing and storing data, evaluating, and analyzing data (Boon Ng, 2019). The SCONUL model identifies that the competence to process and use information is performed by learners through comparing and evaluating information, organizing, and synthesizing information (Bainton, 2001). Bundy (2004) specifies corresponding behaviors for the information-processing group, including evaluating information sources, classifying and storing information, and using information to solve problems.

Component 3. Implementing solutions
The solution-implementation component is the group of behavioral expressions typical of problem-solving competence. Students realize solutions, design, and create products to address problems (Watkins, Spencer, & Hammer, 2014). English (2016) identifies several behaviors related to solution implementation, including planning, preparing equipment, designing drawings, assessing feasibility, and fabricating products. In addition, Sen et al. (2018) indicate that the competence to implement solutions is revealed through behaviors such as identifying causes and product shortcomings, making improvements, and guiding development in new contexts.

Component 4. Technical safety
The technical safety component refers to students' technical operation skills that ensure safety rules are followed during the process of product fabrication. This component helps students understand the role of, and how to use, technical tools and equipment, and to observe technical safety rules to protect themselves and others from unfortunate accidents. Based on Boon Ng (2019), manifestations of the technical-practice behavior group include: using equipment (identifying its functions and operating it correctly), maintaining equipment (preserving it and ensuring safety), and calibrating equipment and tools when necessary.

Component 5. Community sharing
The community-sharing component competency helps students empathize with individuals facing difficulties, motivating them to share their learning processes, outcomes, and products with the community. In the STEM education framework of the New York Academy of Sciences, students should be given opportunities to demonstrate the ability to communicate clearly, accurately and persuasively, and to combine multiple modes to convey ideas; furthermore, provision should be made for an environment in which students can discuss and critique STEM arguments (GSA, 2016). Trilling (2009) proposes community-sharing behaviors: presenting ideas in specified formats (reports, written documents, posters, videos…), listening to feedback and developing ideas, and identifying the purpose of sharing. \_\*\*2. 3. Research methods

The study followed the procedure of Boateng et al. (2018) in three phases: item design, scale development, and scale evaluation.

2.3.1. Item design phase

In the item design phase, based on theoretical research, the STEM competency assessment tool for students comprised five components: Information Gathering (TT), Information Processing and Use (XS), Implementing Solutions (TH), Technical Safety (SD), and Information Sharing (CS). Thus, the preliminary questionnaire consisted of 15 behavioral indicators in a Likert format, each containing a behavior-descriptive item and a response section with five levels. The behavioral indicators were coded sequentially from A01 to A15. The Delphi technique was conducted with 21 STEM education experts, including four university pedagogy lecturers and 17 STEM teachers with at least two years of STEM teaching experience at high schools, to refine the content validity of each behavior-descriptive item. Descriptions judged to lack content validity were deleted or replaced, and unclear items were revised. Wording was adjusted to ensure suitability for secondary school students. The measurement instrument resulting from the qualitative study was then used to design the survey questionnaire.

2.3.2. Scale development phase

Next, small-sample quantitative studies were carried out to preliminarily assess the scales of the study constructs. The evaluation criteria for the scale at this step included reliability and validity. Reliability was assessed by the criteria of Cronbach’s Alpha > 0.7 and item-total correlations > 0.3 (Nunnally & Bernstein, 1994). The convergent validity of the scale was evaluated via factor loadings (FL) in exploratory factor analysis (EFA) with a minimum loading threshold of 0.5; a cumulative percentage of explained variance above 50% indicates convergent validity (Gerbing & Anderson, 1988). In addition, for each observed variable, the difference between the maximum loading and other loadings (in absolute value) must be at least 0.3 to ensure discriminant value (Bryant & Yarnold, 1995). The preliminary analyses at this stage help reduce the number of behavioral indicators in the competency assessment tool and extract the number of factors in the structure of students’ STEM competencies.

2.3.3. Scale evaluation phase

Finally, the main study was conducted on a large sample. The results were subjected to evaluation of the remaining reliabilities and validities, as well as examination of the fit of the structural model to the population using AMOS 24.

Evaluation of reliability, convergent validity, discriminant validity

Internal consistency reliability of the scale was determined using two indices: Cronbach’s alpha and composite reliability (CR) (DeVellis & Thorpe, 2021). The larger the Cronbach’s alpha, the higher the internal consistency reliability. Composite reliability ranges from 0 to 1 and is considered acceptable when greater than 0.7.

Convergent validity indicates the degree to which indicators converge or share a common proportion of variance for a construct, meaning that the set of measures for a construct must be highly interrelated to achieve convergent validity (Kline, 2016). This is reflected by the standardized regression weights of each indicator for the latent construct when the scale is unidirectional. For a scale to demonstrate convergent validity, the standardized loadings of its component variables must be greater than 0.5 and statistically significant (Gerbing & Anderson, 1988). Convergent validity is also assessed via the average variance extracted (AVE). An AVE of 0.5 or higher confirms convergent validity (Hair et al., 2019).

Discriminant validity indicates the extent to which a construct is statistically distinct from another construct in terms of correlations and the measurement indicators within each construct (Hair et al., 2019). The traditional approach to assessing discriminant validity is the Fornell–Larcker criterion, which requires that the square root of AVE be greater than the inter-construct correlations. Other evaluation criteria include that the correlation between two constructs should be less than 0.85 and the maximum shared variance (MSV) should be smaller than the AVE (Kline, 2016).

Testing the fit of the students’ competency structural model

First, chi-square/degrees of freedom (χ2/df) is an index to evaluate the fit of the measurement model; values below 5 indicate acceptable fit and values below 3 indicate good fit (Hair et al., 2019). Other commonly used indices cited by Kline (2016) include absolute fit index — GFI (goodness-of-fit index); adjusted GFI by degrees of freedom — AGFI (adjusted goodness-of-fit statistic); relative fit index — CFI (comparative fit index); Tucker–Lewis index — TLI; and the index determining model fit to the population — RMSEA (root mean square error of approximation). Values of GFI, AGFI, TLI, and CFI greater than 0.9 are considered indicative of a model that fits the data well (Hair et al., 2019).

3.2 Study sample

Regarding sample size, according to Hair et al. (2019), the minimum sample size for studies conducting SEM analysis with models of seven research constructs or fewer is 300. This study applied an accumulative random sampling method to collect information from students at nine high schools: Luong The Vinh High School (District 1), Le Hong Phong Specialized High School (District 5), High School for the Gifted – Vietnam National University (District 5), Pham Phu Thu High School (District 6), Nam Sai Gon Secondary and High School (District 7), Nguyen Du High School (District 10), Nguyen Huu Canh High School (Binh Tan District), Gia Dinh High School (Binh Thanh District), and Hoa Sen Secondary and High School (Thu Duc City). Sampling was conducted from December 2021 to early April 2022. After distributing the questionnaires both online and in person to high schools across the city, more than 887 students participated in the survey. After checking and removing unreliable responses and students who had never studied under a STEM-oriented curriculum, a sample of 544 students was selected for analysis.

Through the process of developing and standardizing the instrument, the research results show that not every behavioral indicator is suitable for inclusion in the assessment tool. In addition to the behavioral indicators in the assessment tool being qualitatively and content-wise accurately articulated, they must also meet quantitative criteria for reliability and validity. Some evaluation criteria, if derived solely from theoretical research without quantitative validation, will be highly subjective, making it difficult to ensure accuracy and objectivity when assessors present results and judgments about learners' competencies.

4. Conclusion

Thus, the study developed and standardized a competency assessment tool for high school students in STEM-oriented learning. The revised instrument comprises three components of STEM competencies: Information gathering and processing (IN), Implementing solutions and community sharing (PS), and Using technical equipment and tools (TE), which are assessed using a 7-point Likert scale. The higher the mean score across all behavioral indicators, the greater the students' ability to engage in STEM-oriented learning. Through a rigorous three-stage research process — item/question design, scale development, and scale evaluation — the results of the Competency-based evaluation and modeling (CB-SEM) analysis showed that the developed instrument met the criteria for internal consistency reliability and validity.

ABSTRACT

Nowadays, competency assessment is one of the assessment trends across the globe. Accordingly, the development and standardization of competency assessment tools attract much attention from educators. For STEM education, a standardized and practical STEM competency assessment tool plays a vital role in guiding future development. This study aims to develop and standardize a self-assessment tool for the STEM competencies of students in Ho Chi Minh City. To ensure the tool's reliability and validity, the research was rigorously conducted in three stages: questionnaire design, scale development, and scale evaluation. Quantitative analysis shows that the tool is reliable, valid, and suitable for evaluating the STEM competencies of students in Ho Chi Minh City. Consequently, this tool is appropriate for students to self-assess their competencies and can serve as a competency framework to help teachers design other assessment instruments for STEM learning.
