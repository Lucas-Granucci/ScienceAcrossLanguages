ABSTRACT
Currently, competency-based assessment is a prevailing trend in global education.
Accordingly, the development and standardization of competency assessment tools is of particular interest to educators.
For STEM education, a standardized and contextually appropriate STEM competency assessment tool plays a crucial role in shaping future development.
This study aims to develop and standardize a STEM competency assessment tool for high school students in Ho Chi Minh City as students learn according to the STEM education orientation.
To ensure the reliability and validity of the tool, the research process was rigorously carried out in three phases: question design, scale development, and scale evaluation.
The results of quantitative analysis show that the tool is reliable, valid, and appropriate for evaluating the STEM competencies of local students.
Thus, this toolkit can serve as a means for students to self-assess their competencies or as a competency framework to support teachers in developing other assessment tools during the STEM education-oriented learning process.
Keywords: CB-SEM; competency assessment; high school students; STEM competency Introduction
In recent years, alongside the transition of educational programs toward a competency-based approach, assessment in education has commonly shifted from evaluating the ability to reproduce knowledge to assessing studentsвЂ™ competencies (Shulman, 2008).
Competency assessment not only provides objective comments and conclusions about studentsвЂ™ learning processes and outcomes, but also focuses on identifying strengths and weaknesses within the competency structure of students.
This offers both qualitative and quantitative feedback on the effectiveness of studentsвЂ™ learning processes and teachersвЂ™ instructional practices.
As a result, students can determine appropriate adjustments to enhance their own learning quality, and teachers can identify measures to support studentsвЂ™ continuous progress and development of essential competencies and attributes, thereby preparing them for future endeavors.

The Resolution of the Eighth Conference of the Central Executive Committee of the Party (Term XI) states: вЂњExaminations, tests, and evaluations of educational and training outcomes should be conducted gradually according to advanced criteria that are trusted and recognized by society and the global educational community.
Evaluation results obtained during the learning process should be coordinated with end-of-term and end-of-year assessments; evaluations by teachers should be combined with studentsвЂ™ self-assessments; and school-based evaluations should be integrated with those of families and societyвЂќ (General Secretary, 2013).
Thus, it can be concluded that assessment activities or competency assessments are not merely the responsibility of teachers but also require a combination with self-assessment activities and self-monitoring of learnersвЂ™ own competency development processes.
For STEM education, the role of competency assessment for learners is increasingly affirmed, as tools for assessing and measuring studentsвЂ™ STEM competencies are considered essential for improving the quality of STEM education by providing feedback on the effectiveness of each stage of learning (Arikan, Erktin, & Pesen, 2020).
However, accurately assessing human competency in general, and that of students in particular, is not easy; a rigorous research process is required to ensure both reliability and validityвЂ”the key factors reflecting the quality of a measurement scale (Baartman et al., 2006).
Therefore, educational researchers around the world are continuously striving to combine qualitative and quantitative research methods in the process of developing competency assessment tools.
In Vietnam, most competency assessment tools for students engaged in STEM-oriented education are developed based on theoretical frameworks inherited from international research, without standardization or adjustment to suit the specific characteristics and implementation levels of STEM education in practice using actual data.
This results in difficulties when attempting to conduct systematic assessments or selecting assessment tools for studentsвЂ™ learning processes and outcomes in the context of STEM education.
Therefore, it is essential to develop and standardize a STEM competency assessment tool that ensures reliability, validity, and suitability for the implementation level of STEM education in each locality in Vietnam.
For this reason, we undertook the development and standardization of a STEM competency assessment tool for high school students in Ho Chi Minh City.
Through literature review, in-depth interviews with expert teams, and the collection and statistical analysis of data from 544 high school students who participated in STEM lessons locally, the results indicate that the developed STEM competency assessment tool for students meets standards of reliability, validity, and overall suitability.
2.
Subjects and research methods
2.1.
Competency assessment of students using a standardized assessment tool
2.1.1.
Assessing students based on the competency-based approach
Assessing studentsвЂ™ competencies is an important process that aims to orient the development of studentsвЂ™ competencies.
Competency is understood as an individual attribute that is formed and developed through innate qualities and the process of learning and training, allowing a person to mobilize and synthesize knowledge, skills, and other personal attributes such as interests, beliefs, willpower, etc., in order to successfully perform a specific type of activity and achieve the desired results under certain conditions (MOET, 2018).
Structurally, competency can be modeled as a system composed of components including: competency modules, constituent competencies, and behavioral indicators expressed through the learning process (Binh, 2015).
The competency modules are related to the specialized fields that form the foundation of the competency.
From there, constituent competencies are separated from the overall competency, carrying the distinctive characteristics of the respective specialized fields.
Both of these components are latent factors that cannot be directly assessed; instead, they must be inferred through the expressions of a set of corresponding behavioral indicators over a certain period of time.
Behavioral indicators provide data that inform the assessment of competency throughout the learning process.
Behaviors manifested externally through activities such as writing, speaking, creating, etc., form the basis for measurement to determine the level of competency achieved.
Thus, the essence of competency assessment in students is the process of examining and measuring the extent to which competencies are achieved when students successfully accomplish tasks and solve problems during the learning process, through the collection and analysis of externally observable behavioral expressions (Baartman et al., 2006).

Assessment results help teachers identify studentsвЂ™ strengths and limitations in specific competencies, thereby providing guidance to support students in learning and fostering those competencies.
In addition, competency assessment impacts the deeper levels of studentsвЂ™ cognition, motivating their learning, fostering good habits, nurturing passion, and enabling them to recognize their own values, which in turn directs them toward self-improvement and personal development.
Building and standardizing a competency structure framework becomes an essential criterion for assessing studentsвЂ™ competencies.
Teachers can use the framework to design checklists, rubrics, and tests for competency assessment, as well as to develop appropriate lesson plans that foster and develop studentsвЂ™ competencies.
Furthermore, students can rely on the competency framework to plan for self-monitoring and self-development.
2.1.2.
Criteria for the reliability and validity of standardized competency assessment tools
According to the positivist school, validity and reliability are two fundamental cognitive tools and the basic standards for standardizing an assessment tool, such as a competency assessment tool (Winter, 2000).
The validity of a measurement tool reflects its accuracy in measuring the intended object.
Specifically, for an assessment tool to be considered valid, it must meet criteria such as content validity, measurement validity, and construct validity.
Content validity reflects the degree of accuracy regarding the expression of the measurement toolвЂ™s content.
Thus, a measurement tool needs to be standardized in terms of content through clear, focused, and concise expression, and for effective content revision, the tool should be reviewed for presentation by field experts using the Delphi research method.
Measurement validity is determined through convergent validity and discriminant validity.
Convergent validity indicates the degree of correlation between measurement variables (behavioral indicators) within the same component.
Discriminant validity demonstrates the distinction between these competency components and other components within the same competency structure.
In quantitative research on competency measurement tools, convergent validity is determined by the level of correlation among behavioral indices, while discriminant validity is examined by assessing whether the components within a competency are truly distinct from one another.
Construct validity relates to the extent to which the tool measures the concepts, behaviors, ideas, and qualities of the theoretical structure.
In competency assessment, by measuring and testing construct validity, the researcher can model the theoretical structure, accept, adjust, or propose new structures suitable to quantitative results.
On the other hand, to present the concept of reliability, Joppe (2000) expressed it as follows: вЂњThe extent to which results are consistent over time and represent an accurate depiction of the total population under study is referred to as reliability.
If the research results can be replicated under a similar methodology, the measuring instrument is considered reliable.вЂќ Thus, the reliability of a competency assessment tool can be understood as the degree of stability of that tool.
The reliability of a scale must satisfy the following criteria: stability; equivalence; and internal consistency.
The stability of a competency assessment tool is demonstrated in that the measuring instrument must ensure stability over time across equivalent samples.
That is, if a scale is tested on sample groups with the same characteristics over an appropriate period of time, the scale will yield similar results.
Specifically, when checking the stability of a competency assessment tool, it is necessary to conduct a preliminary survey with a small sample and a widespread survey to eliminate incompatible variables.
The equivalence of the measurement tool is examined with groups of experts.
If each member in the expert group shares the same viewpoint about the tool, then the measurement instrument ensures equivalence.
The equivalence of the competency assessment tool is validated by gathering expert opinionsвЂ”variables that achieve high agreement are retained, while those showing differences in opinions are considered for adjustment.
Internal consistency among subgroups within the sample, when measured by a single instrument, must yield consistent results.
The sample is divided into smaller groups that collectively represent the entire dataset; measurement is carried out on each subgroup, and the results obtained must match.
If any inconsistencies arise, adjustments need to be considered.

STEM competency of students 2.2.1.

STEM education is an educational model based on an interdisciplinary approach, enabling students to apply scientific, technological, engineering, and mathematical knowledge to solve practical problems in specific contexts (MOET, 2018).
To assess the impacts of this educational model on students, Boon Ng (2019) studied studentsвЂ™ STEM competency within the framework of STEM-related subjects, as well as subjects not directly related to STEM (such as language, culture, literature, etc.).
The research results show that when learning STEM subjects, students exhibit characteristic behaviors from the fields of science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (Boon Ng, 2019).
During the learning process, students approach learning tasks in the manner that scientists, mathematicians, and engineers solve problems and conduct research.
Students actively explore knowledge through activities such as searching, analyzing, and synthesizing information, utilizing knowledge to serve learning objectives.
When participating in STEM lessons, students identify and examine issues related to real-world situations.
Drawing on accumulated knowledge and skills, students propose and select optimal solutions, perform technical operations to create products or models.
Throughout the learning process, students engage in group discussions to present their ideas, contribute to lesson development, and defend their groupвЂ™s viewpoints through critical discussion.
Students also share the process, outcomes, and products of their projects with others.
Based on this analysis, within the scope of this article, studentsвЂ™ STEM competency is understood as the ability to mobilize an integrated set of knowledge and skills in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics to solve practical problems in specific contexts, thereby creating value for both individuals and the community.
2.2.2.
Theoretical Model of StudentsвЂ™ STEM Competency
The structure of studentsвЂ™ STEM competency is developed based on the analysis and synthesis of recurring competency components found in 13 global studies on STEM competency frameworks.
The STEM competency structure comprises five components:
Component 1.
Information Gathering
The information gathering competency component is understood as the ability to identify the necessary information to search for, to locate information sources, and to record information according to initial needs and objectives.
BundyвЂ™s (2004) research indicates that the information gathering competency component helps learners recognize the need to explore information, determine the required information level, and access information effectively.
According to BaintonвЂ™s (2001) SCONUL information skills framework, behavioral indicators of the information gathering competency component include: recognizing the need for information gathering, searching for information sources, developing information search strategies, locating, and accessing information.
The information gathering competency component can be observed in behaviors such as: listening and answering questions, observing, searching for information sources, investigating and exploring, collecting information (planning and recording data), and making conclusions/decisions (Boon Ng, 2019).
Component 2.
Processing and using information
The group of behaviors related to processing and using information within STEM competency encompasses such actions as synthesizing data, comparing data, classifying data, managing and storing data, evaluating, and analyzing data (Boon Ng, 2019).
The SCONUL model identifies information processing and utilization competency as performed by learners through comparing, evaluating information, organizing, and synthesizing information (Bainton, 2001).
Bundy (2004) identifies the behaviors corresponding to the group of behaviors related to processing information, including: evaluating information sources, classifying, storing information, and using information to solve problems.
Component 3.
Implementing Solutions The competency component of implementing solutions consists of typical behavioral indicators of problem-solving competency.
Students implement solutions, design, and create products to address problems (Watkins, Spencer, & Hammer, 2014).
English (2016) identifies several behavioral indicators related to the competency of implementing solutions, including planning, preparing tools, designing diagrams, assessing feasibility, and creating products.
In addition, the study by Sen et al.
(2018) indicates that competency in implementing solutions is manifested through behaviors such as identifying causes and shortcomings of a product, making improvements, and providing development orientations in new contexts.
Component 4.
Technical Safety
The technical safety competency component refers to studentsвЂ™ ability to demonstrate technical skills while adhering to safety rules during the process of creating products.
This competency enables students to understand the role and usage of technical tools and equipment, and to pay attention to technical safety rules to protect themselves and others from unfortunate accidents.
Based on the research of Boon Ng (2019), the indicators of the technical practice behavior group include: using equipment (identifying functions and using them correctly), maintaining equipment (preserving and ensuring safety), and calibrating devices and tools when necessary.
Component 5.
Community Sharing Competency
The community sharing competency enables students to empathize with those facing difficulties and motivates them to share their learning processes, outcomes, and products with the community.
In the STEM education framework of the New York Academy of Sciences, it is required to provide opportunities for students to demonstrate their ability to communicate clearly, accurately, and persuasively, as well as to utilize multiple methods of conveying ideas; furthermore, the framework calls for an environment that enables students to discuss and critically evaluate STEM arguments (GSA, 2016).
Author Trilling (2009) proposes community sharing behaviors: presenting ideas in predefined formats (such as reports, documents, posters, videos, etc.
), listening to feedback and developing ideas, and identifying the purpose of sharing.
2.3.
Research Methods
The study applies the process of Boateng et al.
(2018), consisting of three phases: item design, scale development, and scale evaluation.
2.3.1.
Question Design Phase
In the question design phase, based on theoretical research, the STEM competency assessment tool for students consists of five components: Information Gathering (IG), Information Processing and Utilization (IP), Solution Implementation (SI), Technical Safety (TS), and Information Sharing (IS).
Accordingly, the preliminary questionnaire includes 15 Likert-type behavioral indicators, each comprising a behavioral description and an optional feedback section with five response levels.
The behavioral indicators are coded in sequential order from A01 to A15.
The Delphi technique was conducted with 21 STEM education experts, including 4 university lecturers of education and 17 STEM teachers with at least 2 years of experience teaching STEM at high schools, to validate the content value of each student behavior descriptor.
Descriptions that were assessed as lacking content validity were deleted or replaced, and items that were unclear were revised.
The language was articulated to ensure its appropriateness for high school students.
The results of the scale after qualitative research will be used to design the survey questionnaire.
2.3.2.
Scale development phase
Subsequently, quantitative studies on a small sample size will be conducted to preliminarily evaluate the scales of the research concepts.
The evaluation criteria for the scales at this step include reliability and validity.
Reliability is assessed using CronbachвЂ™s Alpha coefficient, which must exceed 0.7, and item-total correlation coefficients greater than 0.3 (Nunnally & Bernstein, 1994).
Convergent validity of the scale is evaluated through the factor loading (FL) in exploratory factor analysis (EFA), with a minimum factor loading threshold of 0.5; if the cumulative explained variance percentage exceeds 50%, the scale achieves convergent validity (Gerbing & Anderson, 1988).
Additionally, for each observed variable, the difference between the maximum factor loading and the other factor loadings (in absolute value) must be at least 0.3 to ensure discriminant validity (Bryant & Yarnold, 1995).
Preliminary analyses at this stage help to reduce the number of behavioral indicators in the competency assessment tool and to extract the number of components in the STEM competency structure of students.
2.3.3.
Scale evaluation phase
Finally, the main study was conducted on a large sample size.
The results obtained would undergo further evaluation of the remaining aspects of reliability and validity, as well as an assessment of the structural modelвЂ™s overall fit using AMOS 24 software.
пЃ¶ Evaluation of Reliability, Convergent Validity, and Discriminant Validity
Internal consistency reliability of the scale is determined based on two indices: CronbachвЂ™s alpha coefficient and composite reliability (CR) (Devellis & Thorpe, 2021).
The higher the CronbachвЂ™s alpha coefficient, the higher the internal consistency reliability.
Composite reliability ranges from 0 to 1 and is considered good when its value exceeds 0.7.
Convergent validity indicates the degree of convergence or shared proportion of common variance among indicators for a construct, meaning that to achieve convergent validity, the set of variables measuring a research construct should be highly correlated (Kline, 2016).
This is reflected through standardized regression coefficients for each variable of the latent construct if it is a unidimensional scale.
For a scale to achieve convergent validity, the standardized coefficients of its component variables must be greater than 0.5 and statistically significant (Gerbing & Anderson, 1988).
In addition, convergent validity is also evaluated through the average variance extracted (AVE).
An AVE coefficient greater than or equal to 0.5 confirms convergent validity (Hair et al., 2019).
Discriminant validity indicates the extent to which a construct is statistically distinct from another construct, both in terms of correlation and measurement variables within each construct (Hair et al., 2019).
The traditional approach for assessing discriminant validity is the Fornell-Larcker criterion, defined by the condition that the square root of AVE must be greater than the inter-construct correlations.
Additionally, another assessment criterion is that the correlation between any two constructs must be less than 0.85, and the maximum shared variance (MSV) must be less than the AVE coefficient (Kline, 2016).
пЃ¶ Testing the goodness-of-fit of the studentsвЂ™ competency structural model
First, the chi-square/degrees of freedom ratio (П‡ВІ/df) is an index used to evaluate the fit of the measurement model, with values less than 5 indicating acceptable fit, and values less than 3 regarded as good (Hair et al., 2019).
In addition, other commonly used indices following Kline (2016) are important, including: the absolute fit index вЂ“ GFI (goodness-of-fit index); the adjusted GFI according to the modelвЂ™s degrees of freedom вЂ“ AGFI (adjusted goodness-of-fit statistics); the comparative fit index вЂ“ CFI (comparative fit index); the Tucker-Lewis index вЂ“ TLI (Tucker-Lewis index); and the index assessing the modelвЂ™s fit relative to the population вЂ“ RMSEA (root mean square error of approximation).
The GFI, AGFI, TLI, and CFI indices, when greater than 0.9, indicate that the model fits the empirical data well (Hair et al., 2019).
An RMSEA value of up to 0.08 shows a good fit, and an RMSEA less than 0.03 is considered excellent.

Research Sample
Regarding sample size, according to Hair et al.
(2019), for studies employing SEM analysis and models with up to 7 research constructs, the minimum required sample size is 300.
The present study adopted a cumulative random sampling method to collect information from students at 9 high schools, including: Luong The Vinh High School (District 1), Le Hong Phong High School for the Gifted (District 5), High School for the Gifted вЂ“ Vietnam National University (District 5), Pham Phu Thu High School (District 6), Nam Sai Gon Secondary & High School (District 7), Nguyen Du High School (District 10), Nguyen Huu Canh High School (Binh Tan District), Gia Dinh High School (Binh Thanh District), and Hoa Sen Secondary & High School (Thu Duc City).

3.3.
Preliminary evaluation of the reliability and validity of the scale
Before conducting CB-SEM to test the measurement model of studentsвЂ™ STEM competency, exploratory factor analysis (EFA) was performed to reduce or eliminate behavioral indicators that were not suitable for the competency scale and to determine the number of competency components in the structure of studentsвЂ™ STEM competency.
The preliminary analysis results showed that variable A01 from the Information Gathering scale and A06 from the Information Processing and Utilization scale had factor loading differences between factor groups of less than 0.3, indicating that these variables did not ensure discriminant validity and were thus eliminated.
In addition, the preliminary EFA revealed three competency components instead of the original five components.
Recognizing that studentsвЂ™ behavioral indicators still follow the learning process oriented by STEM education, we renamed the components as: Information Gathering and Processing (IN), Solution Implementation and Community Sharing (PS), and Use of Technical Equipment and Tools (TE).
The behavioral indicators were also recoded accordingly: A02, A03, A04, and A05 were replaced by IN01, IN02, IN03, and IN04, respectively; A07, A08, A09, A10, A11, and A12 were replaced by PS01, PS02, PS03, PS04, PS05, and PS06, respectively; and A13, A14, and A15 were replaced by TE01, TE02, and TE03, respectively.

ABSTRACT
Nowadays, competency assessment is one of the assessment trends across the globe.
Accordingly, the development and standardization of competency assessment tools attract much attention from educators.
For STEM education, a standardized and practical STEM competency assessment tool plays a vital role in guiding development in the future.
This study aims to develop and standardize a tool for self-assessing STEM competencies of students in Ho Chi Minh City.
To guarantee the reliability and validity of the tool, the research process was strictly carried out in three stages, including questionnaire design, scale development, and scale evaluation.
Quantitative analysis results show that the tool is reliable, valid, and appropriate to evaluate the STEM competency of students in the region.
Thereby, this tool is suitable for students to self-assess their competency or as a competency framework to support teachers in formulating other assessment tools in STEM learning.
