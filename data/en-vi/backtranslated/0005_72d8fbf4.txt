Abstract.
The buckling response calculation of cracked nanoplates has received a lot of attention recently, especially considering the variable thickness plate and flexoelectric effect.
The finite element formulations are derived from Mindlin's first-order shear deformation theory, and the crack is simulated using phase-field parameters in accordance with phase-field theory.
This is a highly adaptable crack structural approach that offers several advantages over other solutions.
The computational theory's dependability is established by comparisons to published findings.
On that premise, this study investigates the effect of various material and geometrical parameters on the buckling response of a plate with variable thickness according to both linear and nonlinear principles.
These are remarkable research findings, which clearly demonstrate the simultaneous influence of the flexoelectric effect and the thickness variation law of the plate on both the critical buckling load and the critical buckling mode shape of the structure.
Numerical results show that, when cracks appear, the nanoplates become destabilized earlier; however, when the flexoelectric coefficient increases, the plates exhibit greater stiffness and can withstand stronger forces.
This study provides a scientific foundation to assist designers and manufacturers of nanoplates in making recommendations to users when cracks appear.
Keywords: Variable thickness, flexo, nanoplates, FEM.

Abstract.
The buckling analysis of nanoplates with cracks has recently attracted considerable attention, especially with respect to nanoplates with variable thickness and the flexoelectric effect.
The finite element formulation is established based on Mindlin's first-order shear deformation theory, and the crack is modeled using the phase-field theory via the phase-field parameter; this is a very flexible approach for analyzing cracked structures and has many advantages over other methods.
The reliability of the computational theory is verified through comparisons with published results.
On that basis, the paper investigates the influence of several material and geometric parameters on the stability response of the plate, in which the thickness varies according to both linear and nonlinear laws.
These are interesting research results that clearly demonstrate the simultaneous influence of the flexoelectric effect and the variation law of plate thickness on the critical buckling load and buckling modes of the nanoplate.
Numerical results show that when a crack appears in the nanoplate, it becomes unstable more quickly; conversely, increasing the flexoelectric coefficient makes the plate stiffer and better able to withstand loads.
This research establishes a scientific basis to help designers and manufacturers of nanoplates provide recommendations for use when cracks appear in the plates.
Keywords: variable thickness, flexo, nanoplates, finite element method.

1.
INTRODUCTION
Nowadays, nano-sized plate structures are increasingly being used in practice.
To enhance the efficiency of these nano-scale structures, consideration is also given to the impact of the flexoelectric effect, which is the combined effect of the piezoelectric phenomenon and charge polarization caused by the variation of strain components.
As a result, there have been numerous scientific studies investigating the mechanical behavior of such structures.
Zhang and his research team [1] studied the influence of the flexoelectric effect on the electro-elastic response and free vibration of nanoplates based on the classical plate theory and the Ritz method.
Yang and colleagues [2] also utilized the classical plate theory to provide an exact solution for the mechanical response of nanoplates considering the flexoelectric effect.
Shingare and Kundalwal [3] investigated the behavior of graphene-reinforced nanoplates, taking into account the flexoelectric effect, also employing the classical plate theory as the basis for their analytical solutions in this study.
Amir and colleagues [4] used the Navier solution to conduct a study on multilayered sandwich plates, taking into account the influence of the flexoelectric effect.
Ghobadi and his team [5] presented a study on the nonlinear mechanical behavior of nanoplates with graded material properties, taking into account the flexoelectric effect.
Arani and his collaborators [6] used the differential quadrature method to provide a solution for the mechanical response problem of annular nanoplates considering the flexoelectric effect.
Yue [7] investigated the surface effect of the electrodes of nanoplates, additionally considering the flexoelectric effect.
Wang and Xian-Fang [8] based their analysis on the classical plate theory and analytical solutions to investigate the effects of both static and dynamic flexoelectric effects on the response of nanoplates.
Studies [9-16, 33-34] have also clearly demonstrated the mechanical responses of nanoplates subjected to various types of mechanical, thermal, and electrical loads, while considering the influence of the flexoelectric effect.
In practice, structures with variable thickness may be encountered and are used in devices such as sensors and nanogenerators.
However, during fabrication, cracks may develop, altering the mechanical response of the plate.
Therefore, the research findings presented in this paper can serve as a basis for comparison and validation against experimental methods to assess whether cracks have appeared in the plate.
Furthermore, when cracks do occur, these results can help determine up to what crack length the plate remains serviceable under in-plane compressive loading.
This research also serves as a foundation for further studies aimed at predicting crack propagation during loading.
To simulate cracks, various methods are employed, such as the finite element method, the extended finite element method, the isoparametric method, the finite element method combined with the phase-field theory, and the isogeometric method, among others.
Among these, the finite element method combined with the phase-field theory is a novel approach with several advantages, such as high flexibility and the ability to model cracks with complex geometries.
The application of this method to solve mechanical problems has been presented in studies [19-28], which also clearly demonstrate the advantages of phase-field theory and its applicability to plate and shell problems containing cracks.

To simulate the crack, the article employs the phase-field theory [18–27], in which the phase-field variable s varies smoothly and continuously from 0 to 1.
When s = 1, the material is undamaged; when s = 0, the material is fully damaged.
For 0 < s < 1, the material is in a softened state, meaning it is in the process of transforming into a damaged state within a very narrow range.
Therefore, by using the variable s, the crack is represented by a narrow and continuous region, with the width of this region described by the parameter lc.
The energy of the structure is multiplied by s² to reflect the reduction in energy as the crack appears.
At this point, the energy of the nanoplate, taking into account the flexoelectric effect and in-plane compression in the midplane of the plate, is expressed as follows:
\Pi_e = \int_V \left( \frac{1}{2} \varepsilon^T \mathbf{C} \varepsilon + \frac{1}{2} \gamma^T \mathbf{G} \gamma + \frac{1}{2} (\nabla w)^T \mathbf{F}_w (\nabla w) + (\nabla \beta)^T \mathbf{F}_\beta (\nabla \beta) - w^T \chi \sigma \right) dV
where \(\sigma\) is the compressive force acting within the midplane of the plate, \(G_C\) is the critical energy release rate, as specified by the Griffith theory, and:
\nabla w = B_w d, \quad \nabla \beta = B_\beta d,
with:
B_w = \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \quad \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \right], \quad
B_\beta = \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \quad \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \right].
Rewriting expression (14) in terms of nodal displacements, we obtain:
\Pi_e = \int_V \left( d^T B^T D B d + d^T B^T \sigma B d \right) dV.
Additionally,
\int_\Omega \left[ \frac{G_C l_s}{2} (\nabla s)^2 + \frac{G_C}{4l_s} (1 - s)^2 \right] dz d\Omega
where \(\Omega\) is the domain of the plate, and \(s\) is the phase-field variable.
To derive the equilibrium equations of the element, we take the variation of the functional \(\Pi_e(d, s)\):
\frac{\partial \Pi_e}{\partial d} = 0, \quad \frac{\partial \Pi_e}{\partial s} = 0
Written in matrix form:
\int_\Omega B_s^T C_s B_s s d\Omega + \int_\Omega \frac{G_C l_s}{2} (\nabla s)^2 d\Omega = 0
(K_e + \lambda K_G^e) d = 0,
where \(B_s\) is related to the phase-field variable gradient.
After assembling the matrices and applying boundary conditions, the equilibrium equation for the entire structure is as follows:
\sum_e \left( \int_{\Omega_e} B_s^T C_s B_s s d\Omega + \int_{\Omega_e} \frac{G_C l_s}{2} (\nabla s)^2 d\Omega \right) = 0
\sum_e (K_e + \lambda K_G^e) d = 0
Clearly, the stiffness matrix of the element depends on the coefficient \(f_{14}\).
Therefore, the flexoelectric effect (through \(f_{14}\)) clearly influences the load-bearing capacity of the plate, i.e., it affects the stability of the plate.
By solving equation (19a), the phase-field variable s can be obtained, which is then substituted into equation (19b) to determine the critical buckling load as well as the corresponding buckling modes of the nanoplate.

5.
CONCLUSION
This paper presents a study on the static stability of cracked nanoplates, taking into account the flexoelectric effect.
Finite element formulations are developed based on the first-order shear deformation theory and phase-field theory, with the plate thickness assumed to vary according to both linear and nonlinear laws.
Comparative results are carried out to verify the reliability of the computational theory.
On this basis, the paper investigates the influence of several factors, such as the flexoelectric effect, crack length, and boundary conditions, on the static stability response of nanoplates.
The computational results indicate that increasing the coefficient f14 makes the nanoplate stiffer and enhances its load-bearing capacity.
As the crack length increases, the plate becomes softer, and its load-bearing capacity decreases.
Boundary conditions significantly affect both the value of the critical buckling load and the instability modes of the nanoplate.
These computational results serve as valuable reference material for the design, fabrication, and practical use of nanoplates.
