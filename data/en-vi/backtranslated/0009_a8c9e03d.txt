Abstract.
The split feasibility problem is a convex minimization problem.
It is an active field due to its effective applications in image restoration and signal processing.
In this paper, by combining the inertial technique and the gradient projection method, we proposed a CQ projection algorithm integrated with the conjugate gradient method to solve the split feasibility problem in Hilbert spaces.
Polyak’s step size is employed to enhance computational efficiency in large-scale scenarios.
Unlike existing conjugate gradient projection methods, our new approach is designed so that the algorithm requires only one evaluation of the objective function and one evaluation of the gradient at each iteration.
Under suitable conditions, we demonstrate that the algorithm converges weakly to a solution of the problem.
Our method extends and improves upon several recent results in this area.
As an illustrative application, we evaluate the performance of our method on a signal processing problem using synthetic data.
The results of numerical simulations in the sparse recovery problem, as well as comparisons with well-known algorithms, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
Keywords: split feasibility problem, conjugate gradient method, Polyak’s step size, CQ method, weak convergence, sparse recovery problem

Abstract.
The split feasibility problem is a convex optimization problem that has been actively investigated due to its effective applications in image processing and signal processing.
In this paper, we propose a CQ-type projection algorithm combining the conjugate gradient method to solve split feasibility problems in Hilbert spaces.
Polyak’s step size is utilized to reduce runtime in high-dimensional cases.

Our method extends and improves upon several recent results in this line of research.
Numerical results on sparse recovery problems and comparisons with existing algorithms demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

Let 𝐶 and 𝑄 be nonempty closed convex subsets of real Hilbert spaces ℋ1 and ℋ2, respectively.
Without loss of generality, in these Hilbert spaces, we denote the inner product by 〈·,·〉.

The split feasibility problem (SFP) is the problem of finding a point 𝑥∗∈𝐶 such that 𝐴𝑥∗∈𝑄.
The solution set of the problem is denoted by 𝛺, that is,
𝛺 = {𝑥* ∈ 𝐶 : 𝐴𝑥* ∈ 𝑄}.
The split feasibility problem, first introduced by Censor and Elfving [1] in 1994, has numerous applications in signal processing, image processing, and so on (see, for example, [1–12]).
Specifically, consider a linear transformation as follows: 𝑦 = 𝐴𝑥, where 𝐴 is a sampling matrix of size 𝑀 × 𝑁, the vector 𝑥 is the original signal of size 𝑁, and 𝑦 is the measured vector of size 𝑀.
Typically, 𝑀 is much smaller than 𝑁.
How can we recover 𝑥 knowing 𝑦 and 𝐴?
It is known that, in this case, there are 𝑀 equations to solve for 𝑁 unknowns, and since 𝑀 is less than 𝑁, there will not be a unique solution.
This scenario can be illustrated by the Discrete Fourier Transform, in which 𝑦 consists of only 𝑀 frequencies out of the total 𝑁 frequencies from the full Fourier transform.
The question is whether it is possible to recover the signal 𝑥 from only 𝑀 frequency samples of the measured signal 𝑦.
Remarkably, E. Candes, J. Romberg, and T. Tao in [13] have shown that the answer is affirmative in the case where 𝑥 is a sparse signal, i.e., 𝑥 of length 𝑁 contains only 𝑙 nonzero elements.
For such a sparse signal 𝑥 of length 𝑁, in which 𝑙 elements are nonzero and the remaining 𝑁 − 𝑙 elements are considered to be zero, the measurement vector 𝑦 can be obtained via the sampling matrix 𝐴 as follows:
𝑦 = 𝐴𝑥 + 𝜂,
where 𝜂 is a Gaussian noise function with zero mean and variance 𝜎².
To recover the original signal 𝑥̅, the following minimization problem is considered:
𝑥̅ = argminₓ ||𝑥||₁ subject to ||𝑦 − 𝐴𝑥||₂² ≤ 𝜀.
(2)
It is known that problem (2) is equivalent to the following problem:
𝑥̅ = argminₓ ||𝑦 − 𝐴𝑥||₂² subject to ||𝑥||₁ ≤ 𝑡, (3)
where 𝑡 is an appropriate positive real number.
If we set 𝐶 = {𝑥 ∈ ℍ₁ : ||𝑥||₁ ≤ 𝑡}, 𝑄 = {𝑦}, then solving problem (3) can be reduced to solving the SFP (1).
The split feasibility problem was originally studied by Censor and Elfving in finite-dimensional spaces and was later investigated by many other mathematicians in infinite-dimensional spaces (see, for example, [2,5,6,8,9,11,12,14-19] and the references therein).
The most common iterative method for solving the SFP is the CQ algorithm proposed by Byrne in [3].
This method is a special case of the projected gradient algorithm in convex optimization problems with optimal value 0:
2‖Ax−PQAx‖
As we know, the function f is convex and differentiable, and ∇f(x) = A*(I−PQ)Ax, x ∈ H₁ is strongly monotone
with constant 1/‖A‖², that is:
⟨∇f(x) −∇f(y), x−y⟩ ≥ 1/‖A‖² ‖∇f(x) −∇f(y)‖² for all x, y ∈ H₁.
However, the CQ algorithm requires knowledge of the norm of the bounded linear operator, which is evidently not easy to compute.
Therefore, self-adaptive methods have been proposed; see, for example, [9,18,20,21].
Notably, López et al.
in [9] developed a step size selection method such that when applying the CQ algorithm, no information about the norm of the operator is required.
Note that the CQ method only achieves weak convergence in Hilbert spaces.
For other interesting results related to the split feasibility problem, refer to the survey by Ansari and Rehan [22] and recent results in [5,7,11,15-17,19].
In 2022, Huang and Liu [7] combined the multi-step gradient algorithm of Kesornprom, Pholasa, and Cholamjiak [8] with the conjugate gradient method of Sakurai and Iiduka [23] to propose a new conjugate gradient algorithm for solving the split feasibility problem (SFP) and presented an application in the field of signal processing.
However, upon carefully studying this paper, we have identified several shortcomings as follows: The computational load is still quite heavy (requiring two evaluations of the function and the gradient); the convergence proof of the algorithm has a gap after formula (19) (it can only be deduced that the iterative sequence is quasi-Fejér monotone, not Fejér monotone as claimed in the paper); and assumption (C4) is not sufficient to guarantee that the quantities 𝑀 and 𝑀̂ are finite.
Based on the above analyses, in this paper we propose a new CQ algorithm with a conjugate gradient direction and prove the weak convergence of this algorithm.
Our algorithm overcomes the limitations of Algorithm 1 in [7], while also providing a unified approach to CQ-type algorithms that use Polyak’s step size, as presented in recent studies such as [9, 18, 24].
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: The first part of Section 2 presents some fundamental results used for proving and analyzing the convergence of the CQ algorithm with a conjugate gradient direction in the subsequent part of this section.
The numerical results for solving the LASSO problem illustrate the effectiveness of our proposed algorithm.
Section 3 is the conclusion of the paper.

CONCLUSION
In this paper, we proposed an inertial CQ algorithm with conjugate gradient direction to solve the split feasibility problem (1) in Hilbert spaces.
The step size is computed using Polyak’s formula, a method that is highly effective when the optimal value is known.
We have shown that the sequence generated by the proposed algorithm converges weakly to a solution of the split feasibility problem.
Our algorithm has improved and extended several CQ methods with Polyak’s step size presented in recent studies.
The numerical results demonstrate its effectiveness in comparison with known algorithms.
