Accurately determining the value and fully identifying the components of loads and actions is a very important factor in structural design calculations. This factor determines the economic and technical requirements of a project and is reflected through the total construction investment as well as constraints related to strength, stability, deformation, and the service life of the structure. In practice, many latent issues and problems arising in construction works are due to an inadequate assessment of loads and actions. In addition, loads and actions do not possess inherent values or consistent modes of effect; instead, they depend on changes in natural conditions and socio-economic factors. Today, we are facing significant changes in loads—not only in their extraordinary modes of action due to socio-economic conditions—but also subject to major variations from natural conditions.
At the same time, as our country is undergoing a process of international integration and globalization, the comprehensive adoption of a technical standards system is inevitable.
In this article, I address some differences in the calculation of loads and actions between TCVN 2737:2020 and TCVN 2737:1995 as applied to civil and industrial construction works.
Keywords: TCVN 2737:1995; TCVN 2737:2020; Loads and actions. The concept of load combination mentioned in this standard can easily lead to misunderstandings, specifically confusing the combination of causes with the correct understanding of the combination of effects. This is because most of the consequential effects are addressed based on the principle of independent action, making it easy to accept minor deformations and displacements in the structure. As a result, the linear summation of effects becomes similar to the linear summation of the causes of those effects.

VALUES OF DEAD LOAD, LIVE LOAD, AND BASIC CHARACTERISTIC PARAMETERS: [1],[2],[3] The TCVN 2737:2020 standard specifies in detail the characteristic parameters used to determine the value of dead load, including the volumetric weight parameters for various types of materials.
For live loads, TCVN 2737:2020 only specifies the total value and does not stipulate a value for the long-term component.
In addition, there are adjustments and additions to live loads for certain functional areas: - The live load for stands is separated and not generally regulated within the group of meeting rooms, dance halls, etc. - Additional live loads are specified for commercial areas, exhibition and display spaces… - Two more live load values are added for the workshop functional group… - Live load for refuge areas in high-rise buildings is added… - Live load for floors in special areas is supplemented… - Live load for technical floors is introduced… - The live load value for the mezzanine functional group is adjusted and one additional value is included… - For the livestock facility group, a specific value is stipulated instead of a lower limit… - The value is adjusted and one additional live load value is added for the functional group of usable flat roofs… - A lower limit value is specified for the live load in the platform group… - Live load is specified for the functional group of waiting rooms, reception and waiting areas in airports. - Values for the parking garage functional group are adjusted and supplemented… - Live load is added for the fire engine group… - Live load is added for the helipad group… Compared to TCVN 2737:1995, in TCVN 2737:2020 the load reduction factor A has been eliminated for loaded areas greater than 9 m2 and is only allowed to be used for loaded areas exceeding 36 m2.
In the TCVN 2737:1995 standard, the reliability factor for crane loads is taken as 1.1.
In TCVN 2737:2020, the adjusted value of Q is 1.5; the recommended combination coefficients are 0i = 0.7 and 2i = 0.3. The additional coefficients used for calculating the strength of crane beams subjected to concentrated vertical loads from crane wheels have also been revised; these coefficients are lower than those in TCVN 2737:1995. In TCVN 2737:2020, the requirements for structural calculations under crane loads are included within the Design Requirements and are not specified in detail as in TCVN 2737:1995.

CONCLUSION: - The TCVN 2737:2020 standard provides more detailed regulations on the structure of action combinations, replacing the simplified concept of load combinations in TCVN 2737:1995. - The TCVN 2737:2020 standard specifies more detailed characteristic parameters for calculating dead load values, including parameters on the bulk density of various material types. - The TCVN 2737:2020 standard adjusts and supplements live load values for functional categories that are now more diverse in practice compared to TCVN 2737:1995. - The TCVN 2737:2020 standard gives a more open approach for calculating wind loads acting on structures; the zoning and intensity of wind pressure are also more thoroughly revised compared to TCVN 2737:1995.

Introduction In recent years, along with the transition to a competency-based approach in educational curricula, assessment in education has shifted from measuring knowledge recall to evaluating learners’ competencies (Shulman, 2008). Competency assessment not only provides objective comments and conclusions regarding students’ learning processes and outcomes, but also focuses on identifying both strengths and limitations within students’ competency structures. This yields both qualitative and quantitative feedback on the effectiveness of students’ learning and teachers’ instruction. As a result, students can find appropriate directions to adjust and improve their own learning quality, while teachers can devise measures to continuously support students’ progress and develop the necessary competencies and qualities to prepare for
Journal of Science, Ho Chi Minh City University of Education Ta Thanh Trung and co-authors adapt to the changing world, contributing to the achievement of the established educational objectives.
The Resolution of the Eighth Plenum of the Party Central Committee (11th tenure) clearly states: “Examinations, tests, and the assessment of educational outcomes must be gradually conducted according to advanced criteria that are trusted and recognized by society and the global education community. The results of assessments during the learning process should be integrated with periodic and end-of-year evaluations; teacher assessments should be combined with students’ self-assessments; and school evaluations should be coordinated with evaluations from families and society” (General Secretary, 2013). Thus, it can be concluded that assessment activities, including competency assessment, are not isolated tasks for teachers alone but also require the combination with self-assessment and self-monitoring of personal competency development by learners themselves.
For STEM education, the role of assessing learners' competencies is further affirmed as tools for evaluating and measuring students' STEM competencies are considered essential for enhancing the quality of STEM education, due to their feedback on the effectiveness of each learning stage (Arikan, Erktin, & Pesen, 2020). However, accurately assessing human competencies in general, and students' competencies in particular, is not easy; it requires a rigorous research process to ensure both reliability and validity—the factors that demonstrate the quality of the measurement scale (Baartman et al., 2006).
Therefore, educational researchers around the world are continuously striving to coordinate between qualitative and quantitative research methods in the process of developing competency assessment tools.
In Vietnam, most tools for assessing students’ competencies in STEM-oriented education have been developed based on theoretical frameworks inherited from international research, without standardization or adaptation to suit the specific characteristics and levels of STEM education implementation in practice using empirical data. This has led to challenges in carrying out consistent assessments or choosing appropriate tools to evaluate students' outcomes and learning processes in STEM-oriented education. Therefore, the construction and standardization of STEM competency assessment tools that ensure reliability, validity, and are appropriate for the level of STEM education implementation in each locality in Vietnam is essential. For this reason, we have undertaken the development and standardization of a STEM competency assessment tool for high school students in Ho Chi Minh City. Through a process of literature review, in-depth interviews with experts, and the collection and statistical analysis of data from 544 high school students who participated in local STEM lessons, the research results have produced a STEM competency assessment tool for students that meets standards of reliability, validity, and overall suitability.

Subjects and research methods 2.1. Assessing students’ competencies with standardized assessment tools 2.1.1. Assessing students using a competency-based approach Assessing learners’ competencies is an important process that guides the development of students’ competencies.
In this context, competency is understood as an individual attribute that is formed and developed through innate qualities and the processes of learning and practice, enabling a person to effectively mobilize an integration of knowledge, skills, and other personal attributes such as interest, belief, willpower, etc., in order to successfully perform a specific type of activity and achieve the desired outcomes under particular conditions (MOET, 2018). Structurally, competency can be modeled as a system comprising components such as competency domains, constituent competencies, and behavioral indicators that are exhibited throughout the learning process (Binh, 2015). The components of competency pertain to specialized fields that constitute the overall competency. From there, constituent competencies are derived from the core competency and carry characteristic features from specialized fields. Both of these components are latent factors that cannot be assessed directly; instead, they must be inferred through the manifestations of a set of corresponding behavioral indicators over a given period of time. Behavioral indicators are data that provide information about competencies, serving as the basis for assessment throughout the learning process. Behaviors manifested externally through activities such as being able to write, speak, or create, and so on, serve as the basis for measurement to determine the level of competency achieved. Thus, the essence of assessing students’ competencies is the process of examining and measuring the degree of competency attained when students successfully carry out tasks and solve problems during their learning, through the collection and analysis of externally observable behavioral manifestations (Baartman et al., 2006).

In addition, competency assessment impacts students’ deeper cognitive levels, motivates learning, forms good habits, nurtures passion, and enables students to recognize their own values, thereby guiding them in self-training and self-improvement. The development and standardization of a competency framework have become essential criteria for assessing students’ competencies. 2.1.2.
Criteria for reliability and validity of standardized competency assessment tools According to the positivist school, validity and reliability are two fundamental cognitive tools and serve as the basic standards for standardizing an assessment tool, such as a competency assessment tool (Winter, 2000). In this context, the validity of a measurement tool reflects its accuracy in measuring the intended subject. Specifically, for an assessment tool to be considered valid, it must meet the following criteria: content validity, measurement validity, and construct validity. Content validity reflects the accuracy of the measurement tool’s content in terms of expression. Therefore, a measurement tool needs to be standardized in terms of content through clear, focused, and succinct expressions, and to improve content validity effectively, the tool should be reviewed regarding its presentation by experts in the field using the Delphi research method. Measurement validity is determined through convergent validity and discriminant validity. Convergent validity indicates the degree of correlation among measurement variables (behavioral indicators) within the same component. Discriminant validity demonstrates the distinction between the competencies of one component compared to another within the same competency structure. In quantitative research on competency measurement tools, convergent validity is determined by the degree of correlation among behavioral indicators, while discriminant validity is examined by determining whether the components of the competency are truly distinct from one another. Construct validity relates to the extent to which the tool measures the concepts, behaviors, ideas, and qualities of the theoretical structure. In competency assessment, by measuring and testing construct validity, researchers can model the theoretical structure, accept, revise, or propose a new structure in line with quantitative findings. On the other hand, to present the concept of reliability, Joppe (2000) described it as follows: “The extent to which results are consistent over time and an accurate representation of the total population under study is referred to as reliability.”
“If the research results can be replicated using the same methodology, then the measurement tool is considered reliable.” Thus, the reliability of a competency assessment tool can be understood as the degree of stability of that tool. The reliability of a scale must satisfy the following criteria: stability; equivalence; and internal consistency. The stability of the competency assessment tool is reflected in its ability to maintain consistency over time across equivalent samples. In other words, if a scale is tested on groups with similar characteristics within an appropriate timeframe, it should yield similar results. Specifically, to test the stability of a competency assessment tool, it is necessary to conduct a preliminary survey with a small sample as well as a large-scale survey in order to eliminate unsuitable variables. The equivalence of a measurement tool is examined with expert groups. If all members of the expert group share the same opinion regarding the tool, then the tool is deemed equivalent. The competence assessment tool’s equivalence is verified by seeking expert opinions; variables with high consensus are retained, whereas variables with differing expert opinions will be considered for revision. Internal consistency means that when the sample is divided into small groups containing the full data set, measurement results across these groups should be consistent; if inconsistencies are detected, revisions should be considered.

Students’ STEM competencies 2.2.1.
The concept of STEM competency HO CHI MINH CITY UNIVERSITY OF EDUCATION JOURNAL OF SCIENCE Vol. 19, No. 8 (2022): 1255-1270 STEM education is an educational model based on an interdisciplinary approach, helping students apply knowledge from science, technology, engineering, and mathematics to solve practical problems in specific contexts (MOET, 2018). To assess the impacts of this educational model on students, Boon Ng (2019) studied students’ STEM competency within the framework of education based on STEM subjects as well as non-STEM subjects (such as language, culture, literature, etc.). Research results show that when studying STEM subjects, students display behavioral expressions characteristic of the fields of science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (Boon Ng, 2019). In the learning process, students approach learning tasks in ways similar to how scientists, mathematicians, and engineers solve problems and conduct research. Students actively explore knowledge through activities of inquiry, analysis, and synthesis, using knowledge for learning purposes. When participating in STEM lessons, students investigate and identify problems related to real-life situations. From the accumulated knowledge and skills, students propose and choose optimal solutions and carry out technical operations to create products or models. Throughout the learning process, students exchange and discuss in groups to present their own ideas, contribute to lesson development, and critically defend their group’s viewpoints. Students share the process, outcomes, and products of their projects with others. Based on the analysis results, within the scope of this paper, students' STEM competency is understood as the ability to mobilize and integrate knowledge and skills in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics to solve practical problems in specific contexts, creating value for both individuals and the community.
2.2.2.
Theoretical model of students’ STEM competency The structure of students’ STEM competency was developed based on the analysis and synthesis of recurring competency components found in 13 studies on STEM competency frameworks worldwide. The STEM competency structure comprises five components: Component 1. Information gathering competency The component of information gathering competency refers to the ability to identify the information needed, locate information sources, and record information according to initial needs and purposes. Bundy (2004) found that information gathering competency helps learners recognize the need to seek information, determine the level of information required, and access information effectively. Based on Bainton’s (2001) SCONUL model of information competency structure, behaviors associated with the information gathering competency include: recognizing the need to collect information, searching for sources of information, developing information search strategies, locating, and accessing information. The information gathering competency can be observed through behaviors such as: listening and answering questions, observing, searching for sources of information, investigating and exploring, collecting information (planning and recording data), and drawing conclusions/decisions (Boon Ng, 2019).
Component 2.
Processing and Using Information The group of behaviors related to processing and using information within STEM competency encompasses actions such as synthesizing data, comparing data, classifying data, managing and storing data, evaluating, and analyzing data (Boon Ng, 2019). The SCONUL model identifies information processing and usage competency as carried out by learners through comparing, evaluating information, organizing, and synthesizing information (Bainton, 2001).
Bundy (2004) identifies the behaviors corresponding to the group of information processing behaviors, including: evaluating information sources, classifying, storing information, and using information to solve problems.
Component 3. Implementing solutions The competency of implementing solutions is a group of typical behavioral manifestations of problem-solving competency. Students actualize solutions, design, and create products to address problems (Watkins, Spencer, & Hammer, 2014).
English (2016) identified several behavioral indicators related to the competency of implementing solutions, including planning, preparing tools, designing drawings, evaluating feasibility, and manufacturing products. In addition, the study by Sen et al. (2018) pointed out that the competency of implementing solutions is manifested through behaviors such as identifying the causes and shortcomings of products, making improvements, and orienting development in new contexts.
Component 4. Technical Safety The technical safety competency component is demonstrated through students’ technical skills to ensure adherence to safety regulations during the process of product fabrication. This competency enables students to understand the roles and proper use of technical tools/equipment, and to pay attention to technical safety rules in order to protect themselves and others from unfortunate accidents.
Based on the research by Boon Ng (2019), the behavioral indicators of the technical practice group include: using equipment (identifying functions and using them correctly), maintaining equipment (preserving and ensuring safety), and calibrating devices and tools when necessary.
Component 5. Community sharing competency The community sharing competency enables students to empathize with individuals facing difficulties, motivating them to share their learning processes, outcomes, and products with the community.
In the STEM education framework of the New York Academy of Sciences, it is required to provide students with opportunities to demonstrate clear, accurate, and persuasive communication skills, as well as the ability to utilize various methods to convey ideas; additionally, it is necessary to create an environment where students can discuss and critique STEM arguments (GSA, 2016).
Trilling (2009) proposes community sharing behaviors: presenting ideas in predetermined formats (reports, documents, posters, videos, etc.), listening to feedback and developing ideas, and identifying the purpose of sharing.
2.3. Research methods The study follows the procedure of Boateng et al. (2018) with three stages: item design, scale development, and scale evaluation.
2.3.1. Question Design Stage In the question design stage, based on theoretical research, the STEM competency assessment tool for students comprises five components: Information Collection (TT), Information Processing and Use (XS), Solution Implementation (TH), Technical Safety (SD), and Information Sharing (CS). Accordingly, the preliminary questionnaire consists of 15 Likert-type behavioral indicators, each including a description of a behavior and an optional response section with five levels. The behavioral indicators are coded in order from A01 to A15. The Delphi technique was carried out with 21 STEM education experts, including four lecturers from pedagogical universities and 17 STEM teachers with at least two years of STEM teaching experience at high schools, to refine the content validity of each student behavior description. Descriptions assessed as lacking in content validity were removed or replaced, and unclear items were revised. The wording was adjusted to ensure appropriateness for high school students. The resulting scale, following this qualitative research phase, would then be used to design the survey questionnaire.
2.3.2.
Scale Development Stage Next, quantitative studies on small sample sizes will be conducted to preliminarily evaluate the measurement scales for the study’s constructs. The criteria for evaluating the scale at this stage include the reliability and validity of the scale. Reliability is assessed based on the criterion that Cronbach’s Alpha coefficient exceeds 0.7 and the item-total correlation coefficient is greater than 0.3 (Nunnally & Bernstein, 1994). The convergent validity of the scale is evaluated through the factor loading (FL) in exploratory factor analysis (EFA), with a minimum acceptable loading of 0.5; if the cumulative percentage of explained variance exceeds 50%, the scale is considered to achieve convergent validity (Gerbing & Anderson, 1988). Moreover, for each observed variable, the difference between the highest loading and the other loadings (in absolute value) must be at least 0.3 to ensure discriminant validity (Bryant & Yarnold, 1995). The preliminary analyses at this stage will help reduce the number of behavioral indicators in the competency assessment tool and extract the number of components in the structure of students’ STEM competency.
2.3.3.
Scale evaluation phase Finally, the main study was conducted on a large-scale sample.
The results obtained will be evaluated for the remaining aspects of reliability and validity, as well as the model fit with the overall dataset using AMOS 24 software.  Assessment of reliability, convergent validity, and discriminant validity Internal consistency reliability of the scale is determined based on two indices: Cronbach’s alpha coefficient and composite reliability (CR) (Devellis & Thorpe, 2021). The greater the Cronbach’s alpha, the higher the internal consistency reliability. Composite reliability ranges from 0 to 1 and is considered good when greater than 0.7. Convergent validity indicates the extent to which indicators share a common proportion of variance for a given concept, meaning that to achieve convergent validity, the set of variables measuring a research construct must be highly correlated with each other (Kline, 2016). This is reflected through the standardized regression coefficients for each variable of the latent construct if it is a unidimensional scale. For a scale to achieve convergent validity, the standardized coefficients of the components must be greater than 0.5 and statistically significant (Gerbing & Anderson, 1988). In addition, convergent validity is also assessed through the average variance extracted (AVE). An AVE greater than or equal to 0.5 confirms convergent validity (Hair et al., 2019). Discriminant validity indicates the extent to which a given construct is truly distinct—statistically—from another construct, in terms of both the correlations and measurement variables within each construct (Hair et al., 2019). The traditional approach to evaluating discriminant validity is the Fornell-Larcker criterion, which requires that the square root of AVE for each construct be greater than its inter-construct correlations. Additionally, another criterion is that the correlation between any two constructs must be less than 0.85 and the maximum shared variance (MSV) must be less than the AVE value (Kline, 2016).
 Testing the fit of the structural model of students' competencies Firstly, the chi-square/degrees of freedom ratio (χ²/df) is an index used to assess the fit of the measurement model, and a value less than 5 is considered acceptable, while a value less than 3 is regarded as good (Hair et al., 2019). In addition, other common indices identified as important by Kline (2016) include: the absolute fit index – GFI (goodness of fit index); the adjusted GFI taking into account degrees of freedom in the model – AGFI (adjusted goodness-of-fit index); the relative fit index – CFI (comparative fit index); the Tucker and Lewis index – TLI (Tucker-Lewis index); the index for assessing the fit of the model to the population – RMSEA (root mean square error of approximation). GFI, AGFI, TLI, and CFI values greater than 0.9 indicate an acceptable fit of the model to the actual data (Hair et al., 2019). An RMSEA value of up to 0.08 suggests a good model fit, and an RMSEA less than 0.03 is considered very good.

The stability analysis of cracked nano-plates has attracted significant attention in recent times, particularly for plates with variable thickness and considering the flexo effect. The finite element formulation is established based on Mindlin’s first-order shear deformation theory, while the crack is modeled using the phase-field theory through a phase-field parameter, making this a highly flexible approach to cracked structures with several advantages over other methods. The reliability of the computational theory is verified through comparisons with published results. On this basis, the paper investigates the influence of several material and geometric parameters on the stability response of the plate, in which the plate thickness varies both linearly and nonlinearly. These are interesting research findings, clearly demonstrating the simultaneous influence of the flexo effect and the law of plate thickness variation on the critical instability load as well as the instability modes of nano-plates. Numerical results show that when a crack appears in the nano-plate, it loses stability more quickly; conversely, increasing the flexo coefficient makes the plate stiffer and more resilient to loads. This study provides a scientific basis to help designers and manufacturers of nano-plates make recommendations for use when cracks are present in the plate.
Keywords: variable thickness, flexo, nano-plate, finite element method.

INTRODUCTION Nowadays, nano-sized plate structures are increasingly used in practice, and to enhance the effectiveness of these nano-scale structures, the flexo effect is also taken into consideration. This effect is a combination of the piezoelectric phenomenon and charge polarization caused by variations in the components of strain. As a result, numerous scientific works have studied the mechanical behavior of these structures. Zhang and his research group [1] investigated the influence of the flexo effect on the electro-elastic response and free vibration of nano-plates based on classical plate theory and the Ritz method. Yang et al. [2] also used classical plate theory to provide an exact solution for the mechanical response of nano-plates considering the flexo effect. Shingare and Kundalwal [3] studied the behavior of graphene-reinforced nano-plates taking into account the flexo effect; starting from classical plate theory, they derived solutions for their research.
Amir and colleagues [4] used the Navier solution to conduct a study on multilayer sandwich plates, in which the effect of the flexo effect was taken into account.
Ghobadi and his colleagues [5] presented a study on the nonlinear mechanical behavior of functionally graded nano-plates, taking into account the flexo effect.
Arani and his collaborators [6] used the differential quadrature method to provide a solution for the mechanical response problem of annular nano-plates considering the flexo effect.
Yue [7] investigated the surface effect of electrodes in nano-plates with the additional consideration of the flexo effect.
Wang and Xian-Fang [8] based their work on classical plate theory and analytical solutions to analyze the effects of both static and dynamic flexo effects on the response of nano-plates. Studies [9-16, 33-34] also clearly demonstrate the mechanical responses of nano-plates subjected to various types of mechanical, thermal, and electrical loads, taking into account the influence of the flexo effect.
In practice, structures with variable thickness may be encountered and used in devices such as sensors or nano-generators. However, during fabrication, cracks may appear, altering the mechanical response of the plate. Therefore, the research results presented in this paper can serve as a basis for comparison and verification with experimental methods to assess whether cracks have formed in the plate. Furthermore, if cracks do appear, the results can help determine up to what crack length the plate may still be usable when subjected to in-plane compressive forces. This research also serves as a preliminary study for further investigations aimed at predicting the propagation of cracks during loading.
To simulate cracks, various methods are used, such as the finite element method, the extended finite element method, isoparametric methods, the finite element method combined with phase-field theory, isogeometric methods, and so on. Among these, the finite element method combined with phase-field theory is a new approach with many advantages, such as high flexibility and the ability to describe cracks with complex shapes.
The use of this method to solve mechanical problems has been presented in works [19-28], which also clearly demonstrate the advantages of phase-field theory and its applicability to plate and shell problems with cracks.

To simulate cracks, this paper uses the phase-field theory [18–27], in which a phase-field variable s varies smoothly and continuously from 0 to 1, where s = 1 indicates that the material is undamaged, and s = 0 means the material is completely fractured. When 0 < s < 1, the material is in a softened state, i.e., it is in the process of being transformed into a fractured state within a very small region. Therefore, by using the variable s, a crack is described by a narrow and continuous region, the width of which is characterized by the parameter lc, and the energy of the structure will be multiplied by the variable s^2 to represent the reduction in energy when a crack appears.

5.
CONCLUSION This paper presents a study on the static stability of cracked nano-plates, taking into account the effect of flexoelectricity. The finite element formulations are developed based on the first-order shear deformation theory and the phase-field theory, with the plate thickness assumed to vary according to both linear and nonlinear laws. Comparative results have been conducted to verify the reliability of the computational theory. On this basis, the paper investigates the influence of several factors, including the flexoelectric effect, crack length, and boundary conditions on the static stability response of nano-plates. The computational results indicate that increasing the coefficient f14 makes the nano-plate stiffer and improves its load-bearing capacity. As the crack length increases, the plate becomes more flexible, and its load-bearing capacity decreases. Boundary conditions have a significant impact on both the critical buckling load value of the nano-plate and its instability modes. These computational results serve as valuable reference material for the design, fabrication, and practical use of nano-plates.

In general education schools, especially at the lower secondary (middle school) level, education and teaching are considered the two main components of the overall educational process. In addition to systematically providing scientific knowledge, the teaching process must also focus on character education, life skills development, and encouraging students to play an active and positive role in activities both within the school and in society.
(Nguyen, 2024).
Experiential activities have become increasingly emphasized since the Prime Minister signed Decision No. 404/QD-TTg dated March 27, 2015, regarding the project for renewing the general education curriculum and textbooks. This project ensures continuity and coherence among educational levels, grades, subjects, thematic studies, and experiential activities (Government, 2015). The importance of experiential activities is also concretized in the 2018 General Education Curriculum, issued together with Circular No. 32/2018/TT-BGDDT dated December 26, 2018, by the Minister of Education and Training (Ministry of Education and Training, 2018). On that basis, various forms of experiential activities are currently being organized for students at lower secondary schools in Bac Lieu City, Bac Lieu Province.
However, the organization of these activities has not been regular, has not been expanded to all grade levels, and has not yet met the students’ needs.
The study presents the results of a survey on the current state of managing experiential activities for students at lower secondary schools in Bac Lieu City, Bac Lieu Province, providing a practical foundation for proposing measures to enhance the quality and effectiveness of managing these activities locally.

Content 2.1.
Concept 2.1.1.
Experiential Activities In education, experience is considered an effective and practical educational trend and approach. Psychologists and educators believe that, in the educational process, experience is regarded as an effective and practical educational trend and approach. This method not only proves effective in developing skills but also helps learners develop character, critical thinking, and problem-solving abilities.
Experiential activities are a form of education that connects knowledge acquired in school with real-life situations. Through individual experiences, students can accumulate additional experience and transform it into practical competence (Dinh, 2014). Experiential activities involve not only students' participation but also the collaboration of stakeholders such as educational institutions, families, and the community, all of whom contribute to specific experiential situations (Nguyen, 2020).
Experiential activities are an educational form designed, oriented, and guided by educators. The goal of experiential activities is to create opportunities for students to engage with real-life situations, experience positive emotions, and integrate their existing experiences with knowledge and skills from various subjects to solve practical problems in school, family, and social life, appropriate to the students’ age.
(Ministry of Education and Training, 2018).
Thus, there are various interpretations of experiential activities. In the broadest sense, experiential activities include content, characteristics, nature, and methods of implementation. The essence of experiential activities is a narrowly defined educational form, designed and organized to enhance students' experience and creativity. The main purpose is to help students not only acquire knowledge but also actively participate in the learning process, thereby developing practical skills and creativity to meet the stated educational objectives.
Journal of Social Sciences and Humanities 2.1.2.
Management of experiential activities for lower secondary school students Managing experiential activities (EAs) in lower secondary schools is a process in which the school principal influences both teachers and students, carried out during and outside regular class hours according to a planned program. The objective of this process is to achieve comprehensive development for students, while also ensuring that experiential activities are organized effectively and closely aligned with the set educational goals.
(Dang, 2020).
2.2.
Theoretical perspectives on the management of experiential activities for lower secondary school students Management is an organized process with objectives and plans, in which the management entity influences the managed object to optimize the use of the organization's resources and opportunities, thereby achieving the set goals. This process takes place within the context of environmental impacts, requiring continuous adaptation and adjustment to achieve the highest possible effectiveness.
(Nguyen et al., 2016).
Educational management is the process by which the management entity purposefully and systematically influences school operations through the implementation of management functions such as planning, organizing, directing, inspecting, evaluating, and adjusting plans in order to coordinate activities within the school. The aim of this process is to make rational use of resources from the state, society, and the school to develop all school activities, with a key focus on teaching and learning, thereby achieving efficiency in attaining educational objectives and plans, and helping the school advance and develop.
(Dang, 2020).
Experiential activities (EAs) are part of the educational process; therefore, the management of EAs is essentially the management of an educational activity. Accordingly, the management of EAs can be defined as a continuous process involving planning, organizing, directing, and monitoring by the management entity toward these activities, based on a system of specific criteria, standards, policies, principles, and methods. The goal of managing EAs is to achieve the set educational objectives and ensure effectiveness in the organization and implementation of experiential activities.
(Tran, 2020).
- Planning experiential activities (EAs): One of the key functions of management is planning, which determines which tasks to prioritize and which to defer in order to avoid being passive regarding time and workload. Having a plan for EAs enables administrators and teachers to access practical settings, social activities, production facilities, the world of occupations, and the labor market, in connection with educating students in qualities, competencies, and career orientation. Developing detailed, specific, and scientific plans for EAs helps principals and those assigned tasks to understand the goals to be achieved and anticipate the strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and challenges in organizing these activities. From there, the necessary resources and measures can be identified to effectively accomplish the tasks. The plan for EAs includes setting objectives, designing programs, identifying implementation steps, and determining the required conditions and means within a certain timeframe to facilitate EAs. The plan must clearly delineate the responsibilities of each department and individual within the organization, as well as specify requirements regarding timing and progress. The plan should reflect that experiential activities are both a compulsory subject and an integrated activity within other subjects and activities of the school.
(Bui, 2023).
- Organizing EAs for students: Implementing the plan for experiential activities requires establishing an EA management apparatus, clearly and specifically assigning and designating responsibilities so that these activities can operate smoothly, uniformly, and in a synchronized manner. The management process of EAs demands the scientific and rational arrangement of tasks and personnel in order to maximize the potential of both individuals and groups. For optimal effectiveness, work assignment should be based on each person’s abilities and strengths, while also taking advantage of favorable conditions and opportunities. At the same time, difficulties and challenges encountered during implementation must be addressed. This stage includes executing the action plan: announcing the activity program plan to all parties participating in EAs, both inside and outside the school, so that each member can proactively fulfill their responsibilities. Task assignments must be precise, ensuring the right people are in the right positions, with clearly defined functions and authorities for each individual and department. Factors such as each person’s capacity, their ability to take on tasks, and resources—such as human resources, finances, facilities, and equipment—all need to be allocated appropriately. Finally, it is essential to establish effective coordination mechanisms among departments and members to ensure the success of the activities.
(Tran, 2020).
- Directing EAs: Directing the implementation of the plan for organizing EAs involves the principal’s impact on the entire process of managing EAs to ensure that these activities are carried out in the right direction, according to plan, and in alignment with the actual conditions of the school and locality, thereby contributing to the comprehensive improvement of educational quality. Human resources involved in EAs must possess the necessary knowledge, skills, and attitudes to be able to guide the development of EA plans, design EA topics, organize EA formats, mobilize participation and coordination among groups, participate in management, administration, organization of activities, and assess the results achieved in terms of students’ qualities and competencies through EAs.
(Chau, 2023).
Inspection and evaluation of the implementation results of EAs: This is the final step in the principal’s management cycle. Evaluation of educational outcomes in EAs is the process of determining the extent to which students have achieved the required qualities and competencies at each stage of learning. The goal of this evaluation is to identify each student’s position, record progress in their personal development, and encourage and guide students to continue improving and perfecting their skills and qualities. Evaluation results not only reflect students’ progress but also serve as an important basis for educational management agencies and teachers to adjust curricula and educational activities within the school to better align with students’ needs and actual circumstances. If attention is paid to the inspection and evaluation of EAs, it will help assess the quality and the competency to organize and implement each EA, providing very useful information for the effective management of activities.
(Dinh, 2014).

Research Methods To survey the current state of experiential activity management for lower secondary school students in Bac Lieu City, Bac Lieu Province, we collected data through questionnaires, interviews, and processed the data using mathematical statistics. The total number of respondents was 139, including 19 administrators (leaders, specialists from the Department of Education and Training, principals, and vice principals of lower secondary schools) and 120 teachers (heads of subject groups, teachers). In addition, several administrators and teachers were interviewed in depth. The survey was conducted from July 2024 to September 2024. The assessment of the extent to which each aspect is manifested was based on the average score (AS) from the survey results, with a range of 0.8 distinguishing each level, calculated using the formula (Max - Min)/n. Specifically, the levels were divided as follows: Level 5 (Good) from 4.2 ≤ AS < 5.0; Level 4 (Fair) from 3.4 ≤ AS < 4.2; Level 3 (Average) from 2.6 ≤ AS < 3.4; Level 2 (Weak) from 1.8 ≤ AS < 2.6; and Level 1 (Poor) AS < 1.8.

General Assessment of the Current Situation Based on the research results and survey of the current situation, it can be seen that the administrators and teachers of lower secondary schools in Bac Lieu City, Bac Lieu Province fundamentally recognize the importance of experiential activities (EAs); the organization of EAs for students plays an important role in helping school unions and teachers achieve set objectives, while also reflecting the actual capacity of administrators and teachers at the school; the direction of EAs for students at lower secondary schools in Bac Lieu City basically meets the requirements; and the inspection and evaluation of EAs for students is given due attention. However, there remain some limitations and shortcomings in the management of EAs. First, a small number of administrators and teachers have not fully recognized the importance of managing EAs for students. Second, planning is still largely formalistic and the management mechanism remains bureaucratic and document-based, lacking hands-on and practical experience. Third, the renewal of methods and organizational forms is still slow; the content is not yet diverse, rich, or attractive, thus the effectiveness is not high, failing to engage students and to build trust among parents. Fourth, there is still a situation in which administrators are neglectful and subjective in the inspection and evaluation of EAs for students. Fifth, coordination among various forces inside and outside the school is not high and there is inconsistency between schools.
3.
Conclusion Through the survey on the current state of managing experiential activities at lower secondary schools in Bac Lieu City, Bac Lieu Province, it can be affirmed that management activities according to the four basic functions (planning, organizing, directing, and inspecting) have all reached a fairly good level. However, there are still many limitations that need to be addressed, especially in the following areas: Enhancing the awareness of administrators and teachers about the role of experiential activities; renovating methods, diversifying content, and increasing the practical relevance in the organization of experiential activities; strengthening inspection and supervision to ensure effectiveness and objectivity; and promoting the coordination between the school and external organizations to better support experiential activities. Based on the evaluation and analysis of the current situation of managing experiential activities for lower secondary school students, we recognize the need for appropriate measures to overcome existing shortcomings and to improve the quality and effectiveness of experiential activities, thereby contributing to enhancing the overall educational quality at lower secondary schools and fulfilling the goal of comprehensive education in the new period.

We evaluated the outcomes of applying SILS in the treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease in newborns. This is a prospective descriptive study conducted on Hirschsprung’s disease patients aged 1–28 days who underwent single-incision laparoscopic surgery at the National Children’s Hospital from January 2020 to December 2021. The results included 23 patients, with a mean age of 22.3±3.2 days (range: 17–28 days). The average operative time was 53.8±11.9 minutes (35–75 minutes), and the average postoperative hospital stay was 4.5±1.1 days (3–7 days). There were 4 cases of enterocolitis, 1 case of anastomotic stricture, and no mortality. This study demonstrates that single-incision laparoscopic surgery is safe and effective in the treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease in newborns.
Keywords: Single-incision laparoscopic surgery, Hirschsprung’s disease, congenital megacolon.
I. INTRODUCTION Hirschsprung’s disease is a developmental disorder characterized by the absence of ganglion cells in the distal gastrointestinal tract, leading to functional intestinal obstruction. Most cases of Hirschsprung’s disease are diagnosed in the neonatal period, with common symptoms including failure to pass meconium within 24–48 hours after birth.1 Surgery is the mainstay of treatment, performed after definitive diagnosis is established by histopathology. The single-incision laparoscopic surgery is conducted through a single umbilical incision using three standard laparoscopic instruments.3 While single-incision laparoscopic surgery has been widely applied in adult patients, its use in pediatric patients, particularly newborns, remains limited. One of the main reasons is the lack of appropriately sized surgical instruments for children, especially newborns.4 Currently, surgeons with advanced laparoscopic skills can still perform this technique by using standard laparoscopic instruments in combination with suspension sutures to ensure effective treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease.5 In 1995, Georgeson was the first to report the application of laparoscopic surgery for Hirschsprung’s disease, showing superior results in terms of reduced trauma, fewer complications, and faster recovery compared to open surgery.2 With significant advances in minimally invasive surgery, in 2010, Muensterer successfully applied single-incision laparoscopic surgery for the treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease. Methods Study design A prospective descriptive study. Convenience sampling was used.
Single-incision laparoscopic surgical procedure: The current consensus among pediatric surgeons worldwide regarding the treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease is early surgery, performed in a single stage using minimally invasive techniques.
The timing of pull-through surgery for newborns is still under debate. Kastenberg's study showed that single-stage pull-through surgery performed during the neonatal period yields postoperative bowel function outcomes comparable to those in older infants.6 Operating on newborns offers several advantages, such as less colonic dilation compared to older children, a thinner abdominal wall, and an easier dissection field; however, postoperative recovery time may be prolonged and there is a higher risk of missing aganglionic segments.6 Single-incision laparoscopic surgery has been successfully performed in newborns with promising results, but the number of studies remains limited and further evaluation of its effectiveness is needed.
Therefore, we conducted this study with the objective of evaluating the outcomes of single-incision laparoscopic surgery in the treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease in newborns.

SUBJECTS AND METHODS Preoperative preparation involved daily colonic irrigation with saline for about 2–3 days prior to surgery, which could be extended depending on the degree of fecal stasis and colonic dilation, aiming to cleanse and reduce the dilation of the colon.
1.
Subjects Technique: The laparoscopic phase involves placing 3 trocars through a single 1.5 cm incision just above the umbilicus, using two 5 mm trocars and one 3 mm trocar (Figure 1); CO₂ insufflation pressure is maintained at 7-8 mmHg. The study included newborn patients diagnosed with Hirschsprung’s disease and undergoing single-incision laparoscopic surgery at the Department of Surgery – National Children’s Hospital during the period from January 2020 to December 2021, meeting the following criteria: Inclusion criteria: Age from 1 to 28 days, meeting the following: (1) Diagnosis of Hirschsprung’s disease based on clinical symptoms, contrast enema showing a transition zone, and intraoperative frozen section pathology confirming the absence of ganglion cells in the narrow segment; (2) Treated by single-incision laparoscopic surgery. A seromuscular colonic biopsy outside the mucosa is performed at two locations: the suspected aganglionic narrow segment and the suspected ganglionic dilated segment; biopsy fragment size is 5 mm. The sigmoid colon and rectum are mobilized along the bowel wall down into the pelvis through the peritoneal reflection for 2–3 cm. The sigmoid artery and superior rectal artery trunks are divided. The colonic mesentery is mobilized proximally to match the length of the aganglionic segment and the portion of colon intended for pull-through, ensuring that the anastomosis is tension-free.

DISCUSSION The aganglionic segment is resected and intestinal continuity is restored by bringing the normal bowel down to anastomose with the anal canal while preserving the function of the sphincter.
With advances in laparoscopic instruments and surgical techniques, minimally invasive treatment methods have become a growing trend for the management of Hirschsprung’s disease, offering advantages such as reduced trauma, minimized bleeding, and improved cosmetic outcomes while still ensuring safety and effectiveness. The timing of pull-through surgery for newborns with Hirschsprung’s disease remains a subject of ongoing discussion.
Surgery in the neonatal period helps prevent preoperative enterocolitis; however, it is necessary to note that Asian children have a high incidence of Hirschsprung’s disease, with a rate of about 3 per 5,000 live births, and males are more affected than females with a male-to-female ratio of approximately 4:1.1 In our study, the male-to-female ratio was much higher than those reported in the literature.
Hirschsprung’s disease usually presents in one of three clinical forms: intestinal obstruction in newborns, chronic constipation in older children, or enterocolitis.
According to the literature, delayed passage of meconium within the first 24 hours or intestinal obstruction during the neonatal period occurs in 80-90% of newborns during this stage.1 All newborns in this study exhibited delayed meconium passage after 24 hours postoperatively, followed by abdominal distention and frequent vomiting; we did not encounter any cases of cecal perforation as previously described in the literature.1 They faced risks such as misdiagnosis, postoperative enterocolitis, and prolonged recovery time after surgery.
Delaying pull-through surgery until after the neonatal period provides additional time for definitive diagnosis. Kastenberg's study showed that single-stage pull-through surgery performed after the neonatal period yields functional outcomes equivalent to those of neonatal surgery. The current global consensus among pediatric surgeons regarding the treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease is early, single-stage surgery using minimally invasive techniques. The absence of ganglion cells in the colonic wall on histopathology is the gold standard for diagnosing Hirschsprung’s disease. Surgical resection of the aganglionic segment is the fundamental principle in the management of this condition.
Swenson first described the surgical technique in 1948.9 To date, although numerous techniques have been developed, the two most common procedures are the laparoscopic-assisted transanal approach by Georgeson and the totally transanal procedure by De La Torre.1,2,10 The objective of surgery is to resect the entire aganglionic segment; in addition, it is essential to ensure that the coloanal anastomosis is tension-free by extensively mobilizing the colon and avoiding twisting of the colon while pulling it down to create the anastomosis. Third, the intraperitoneal colon should be kept intact to prevent contamination of the peritoneal cavity. Fourth, mobilization of the colon and rectum facilitates identifying surgical landmarks and minimizes trauma to the pelvic floor, particularly reducing the risk of overdistension of the anal sphincter during the transanal approach, which could affect postoperative continence.
In 2010, Muensterer was the first to report the application of single-incision laparoscopic surgery to treat Hirschsprung’s disease in six patients with a mean age of 28 ± 32 days, including four newborns. The results showed an average operative time of 145 ± 44 minutes, with no intraoperative complications; the average postoperative period was seven days, and all patients had no visible scars, fed well, had normal bowel movements, and gained weight appropriately.
The author noted that single-incision laparoscopic surgery presents technical challenges during the procedure, but it can still be performed safely through a single umbilical incision, yielding good postoperative outcomes and superior cosmetic results. Three years later, Tang conducted a comparative study on the short-term effects of single-incision laparoscopic surgery versus conventional laparoscopic surgery in the treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease, in which 28 cases were treated with single-incision laparoscopic surgery, with a mean age of 3.4 ± 2.1 months (range: 1–78.8 months) and an average operative time of 122 ± 18 minutes. No intraoperative complications occurred, and the short-term therapeutic effectiveness of the two surgical methods was equivalent.
In 2015, Xia reported a comparison of the mid-term follow-up outcomes between single-incision laparoscopic surgery and conventional laparoscopic surgery in the treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease, involving 40 cases treated with single-incision laparoscopic surgery, with a mean age of 16.9 ± 19.6 months. The application of single-incision laparoscopic surgery for children with Hirschsprung's disease demonstrated efficacy equivalent to that of conventional laparoscopic surgery.
In Vietnam, Nguyễn Thanh Liêm reported the outcomes of applying single-incision laparoscopic surgery for the treatment of 40 children with Hirschsprung’s disease during the period 2013–2015, showing a mean age of 2.7 months (range, 1–17 months); the operative time was 96 ± 23 minutes (range, 50–150 minutes), with negligible blood loss, and the postoperative hospital stay was 4.5 ± 2 days. With an average follow-up period of 65 ± 9.9 months, two patients (5.3%) developed enterocolitis, and there were no cases of fecal incontinence or constipation. All patients were classified as having excellent outcomes according to the Rintala scoring system for bowel continence.5 with an operative time of 226 ± 4 minutes and similar results between the two groups.13 In the treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease, single-incision laparoscopic surgery also shares the general advantages of conventional laparoscopic surgery over the transanal approach alone.
First, endoscopic biopsy allows for determination of the extent of the aganglionic segment before proceeding with colonic mesentery mobilization or rectal resection.

The single-incision laparoscopic surgical technique in our study also utilized conventional laparoscopic instruments, similar to the procedure described in the study by Nguyễn Thanh Liêm, but we did not use supporting suspension sutures.
The mean operative time in our study was 53.8 ± 11.9 minutes, which is much shorter than those reported in other studies both within and outside of Vietnam.5,12-14 This can be explained by the fact that our research group performed the procedure only on newborns, whereas other authors included older children.
JOURNAL OF MEDICAL RESEARCH We found that performing surgery in newborns offers several advantages over older children due to the thinner abdominal wall, which is easier to manipulate, more readily identifiable anatomical structures, less colonic dilation compared to older children, and a thinner intestinal wall because of the absence of recurrent episodes of enterocolitis, making it easier to create the coloanal anastomosis. Single-incision laparoscopic surgery is still considered more challenging to perform, and thus the indications for its use remain more limited than those for conventional laparoscopic surgery. The existing difficulties in performing single-incision laparoscopic surgery include working in less favorable positions for the surgeon, instrument collisions and clashes with the camera, more difficult surgical maneuvers, frequent instrument crossing, and situations requiring the surgeon to operate within a new spatial geometry, deviating from the traditional triangulation principle; access to organs is also more restricted. Therefore, this technique should only be performed by experienced surgeons. Newborns have a thin and highly elastic abdominal wall, and the use of three standard laparoscopic instruments through a 1–1.5 cm skin incision (Figure 1) poses little difficulty in manipulation. For dissection and mobilization of the colon toward the pelvis, our main maneuver is cauterization and blunt dissection close to the rectal wall using a hook electrocautery. However, in cases of abdominal distension or when the aganglionic segment is located high, it becomes more challenging to mobilize the colon at the splenic flexure. In cases of abdominal distension, we usually place a Foley catheter into the descending colon to decompress the colon. We strive to minimize the duration of the laparoscopic phase to limit the effects of CO₂ insufflation; in situations where, during the perineal phase, the colon has not descended sufficiently, we combine perineal traction of the colon with further laparoscopic mobilization up to the splenic flexure or higher if necessary. In this study, the laparoscopic phase lasted approximately 24 minutes (Table 1), and there were no cases where suspension sutures were applied as described in the study by Nguyễn Thanh Liêm.5 Although the length of the aganglionic segment varied, the operative time did not differ, whether the aganglionosis was in the rectum or the sigmoid colon (Table 2). This demonstrates that single-incision laparoscopic surgery is feasible for high aganglionic segments without prolonging the operative time.
Enterocolitis is a common postoperative complication after surgical treatment of Hirschsprung's disease, with an incidence ranging from 30-40%. This complication can occur both before and after surgery, in patients of any age from newborns to adults, and may not necessarily be related to the surgical procedure itself. The mortality rate has significantly decreased over the past 30 years, from 30% to 1%.1 All patients in our study were assessed for postoperative enterocolitis according to the Delphi criteria7, with a score of ≥10 points; our observed rate of enterocolitis (17.4%) was lower than that reported in the literature.7 Colonic stasis combined with immature mucosal immunity is considered a predisposing factor for the development of enterocolitis.7 Some studies suggest that a shorter length of the muscular cuff of the rectum may also reduce the incidence of enterocolitis. In our study, all patients had both the aganglionic segment and the poorly functioning dilated colon segment resected (Figure 2), and a short muscular cuff (~1.5–2 cm) was left in accordance with the modified Soave technique.15 Single-incision laparoscopic surgery is a highly feasible and safe approach that does not require expensive specialized instruments, is cost-effective, and is well-suited to the economic conditions in Vietnam.
However, to date, surgery has proven to be technically feasible, safe, and effective in the treatment of Hirschsprung’s disease in newborns. One case of anastomotic stricture in our study was managed with anal dilation.
This method has few serious complications and is a minimally invasive approach that offers the advantages of laparoscopic surgery.

Introduction Today, modern digital technology is increasingly bringing profound changes to the field of entrepreneurship and business. Meanwhile, Vietnam is a country where the digital economy and the field of entrepreneurship are still in the process of development (Nathan et al., 2022). Therefore, exploring digital technology factors—specifically Fintech—on the formation of intentions to start digital businesses in Vietnam plays an important role for policymakers.
Previous studies have often focused on the intention to start traditional businesses, while research on entrepreneurship in the context of digital transformation remains relatively limited (Ismail et al., 2012). In addition, although technologies have been shown to play an important role in the formation of digital business startups (Nambisan, 2017), previous works on digital entrepreneurship have primarily investigated digital perception (Chan et al., 2017; Zaheer et al., 2019), access to Fintech (Hasan et al., 2023), application of Fintech (Nathan et al., 2022; Stolper & Walter, 2017), or financial literacy (Hasan et al., 2023), without thoroughly analyzing the relationship between various technologies and business, notably Fintech and the intention to start a digital business. Accordingly, Fintech not only affects the way a new business is established, but is also related to shaping a supportive entrepreneurship environment (Alkhalaileh, 2021). Thus, to comprehensively assess the relationship between Fintech and the intention to start a digital business, it is necessary to consider Fintech from an ecosystem perspective, promoting entrepreneurship through both internal factors (knowledge, perception, etc.) and external factors (contextual and environmental support, etc.) (Avarmaa et al., 2022). Therefore, this study applies the theory of planned behavior to examine the impact of the Fintech ecosystem on the intention to start a digital business. The research findings are expected to contribute useful insights for proposing solutions to develop digital business in Vietnam.

Overview and Research Hypotheses 2.1.
Theory of Planned Behavior and the Intention to Start a Digital Business Digital entrepreneurship involves the application of digital technologies to business production or management processes with the aim of creating new value, and these activities take place on the Internet (Kraus et al., 2019).
The Theory of Planned Behavior (Ajzen, 1991) is one of the most well-known theories used to explore individuals’ deliberate behaviors (Maheshwari & Kha, 2022). According to this theory, the intention to perform a behavior is influenced by three factors: attitude toward the behavior, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control. Attitude toward digital entrepreneurship is understood as an individual’s positive or negative evaluation of the act of starting a digital business—the more positive the attitude, the higher their intention to start a digital business. Perceived behavioral control over digital entrepreneurship reflects an individual’s perception of the feasibility of a specific behavior (Ajzen, 1991). When a person perceives that they have sufficient necessary resources to start a digital business, the likelihood of forming the intention to carry out that behavior is higher.
However, the relationship between subjective norms and the intention to start a business remains controversial, with many scholars arguing that subjective norms have an insignificant impact on entrepreneurial intentions in Vietnam (Doanh & Bernat, 2019).
Therefore, this study does not consider the influence of subjective norms.
Based on the above rationale, the following hypotheses are proposed: Hypothesis 1a (H1a): Perceived behavioral control over digital entrepreneurship positively influences the intention to start a digital business among Vietnamese students. Hypothesis 1b (H1b): Attitude towards digital entrepreneurship positively influences the intention to start a digital business among Vietnamese students.

Perception of Blockchain utility – Blockchain technology: By utilizing decentralized networks (blockchains), Blockchain enables simultaneous and secure access to data (Vaigandla et al., 2023). Through the use of blockchain, all stakeholders can access and monitor the stages of production and business products (Wang et al., 2022).
Thus, essential resources such as finance – capital and marketing for conducting business activities are only fully provided through the Blockchain platform.
The use of emerging technologies such as Blockchain can contribute to increasing business efficiency; companies can leverage and innovate to develop products, services, and business processes that not only take advantage of these new capabilities but also create new opportunities and breakthroughs (Festa et al., 2022). Supported by Blockchain technology, Fintech has encouraged hundreds of startups to build services such as mobile payments and international money transfers, notably without any intermediaries (Mukherjee & Pradhan, 2021), thus playing an important role in the global regulation and distribution of finance. Moreover, Blockchain can make relationships more transparent and trustworthy for entrepreneurs and potential investors (Morkunas et al., 2019). Understanding all these benefits of Blockchain thereby fosters entrepreneurial spirit among individuals, contributing to enhanced trust and a positive attitude among entrepreneurs toward the process of starting a business in a complex technological context.
Therefore, the authors propose the following hypotheses: Hypothesis 2a (H2a): Perception of the utility of Blockchain has a positive impact on Vietnamese students’ perceived behavioral control regarding digital entrepreneurship. Hypothesis 2b (H2b): Perception of the utility of Blockchain has a positive impact on Vietnamese students’ attitudes toward digital entrepreneurship. Hypothesis 2c (H2c): Perception of the utility of Blockchain has a positive impact on Vietnamese students’ intention to start a digital business.
Perception of the Utility of Crowdfunding – Internet-Based Community Fundraising: Emerging as a valuable alternative funding source in the search for external financial resources (Belleflamme et al., 2014), crowdfunding through the Internet creates an intermediary environment that connects individuals or newly established companies seeking investment capital for their projects with sponsors who have investment objectives or are interested in the products (Mollick, 2014). One of the major challenges for startups in their early stages is financial difficulty (Acs, 2008). Crowdfunding is considered a financial solution to address barriers in accessing finance and resources (Beltrame et al., 2023). Startups can raise significant amounts of funding from community sources (Lee & Shin, 2018; Hendrikse et al., 2018). Therefore, raising awareness about the operation and application of crowdfunding platforms is a key factor in promoting digital entrepreneurship, especially in developing economies (Phung, 2023).
Accordingly, the proposed hypotheses are as follows: Hypothesis 3a (H3a): Perception of the utility of Crowdfunding positively influences Vietnamese students’ perceived behavioral control over digital entrepreneurship. Hypothesis 3b (H3b): Perception of the utility of Crowdfunding positively influences Vietnamese students’ attitudes toward digital entrepreneurship. Hypothesis 3c (H3c): Perception of the utility of Crowdfunding positively influences Vietnamese students’ intention to start a digital business.
Perception of the utility of Artificial Intelligence (AI): Previous studies have found that AI technology can enhance individuals’ competitiveness, thereby contributing to greater intentions to start a business (Wang et al., 2022). Specifically, through the flexible application of algorithms in accessing, collecting data, and analyzing markets, AI brings about fundamental changes to entrepreneurial thinking by influencing individuals' attitudes toward digital entrepreneurship opportunities (Buxmann et al., 2021). The selective or widespread deployment of AI has the potential to affect both an individual’s decision to start a business and the type of ventures they choose to establish (Chalmers et al., 2021; Hendrikse et al., 2018). Therefore, the perception of AI is considered a powerful facilitator that promotes digital entrepreneurship (Dabbous & Boustani, 2023). When individuals have a higher level of awareness, they perceive greater utility in the technology and consequently develop a more positive attitude toward it (Belanche et al., 2019).
The following hypotheses are proposed: Hypothesis 4a (H4a): Perceptions of the utility of AI positively affect Vietnamese students’ perceived behavioral control over starting a digital business. Hypothesis 4b (H4b): Perceptions of the utility of AI positively affect Vietnamese students’ attitudes toward digital entrepreneurship. Hypothesis 4c (H4c): Perceptions of the utility of AI positively affect Vietnamese students’ intention to start a digital business.

Nowadays, more and more universities are focusing on promoting students’ entrepreneurial mindset by investing in entrepreneurship training programs and upgrading information technology facilities. In addition, the widespread application of digital technologies in higher education helps lay the groundwork for creativity and technology adoption, thereby strongly influencing entrepreneurial intentions, especially the intention to start a digital business (Dabbous & Boustani, 2023; Youseff et al., 2021).

While there is ongoing debate, in general, government support for entrepreneurship aims to promote entrepreneurial spirit and enhance startup activity (Al Halbusi et al., 2023), helping to transform university students’ business passion into intentions and actions related to digital entrepreneurship (Batool et al., 2015). In Vietnam's digital economy, government entrepreneurship support has a positive effect through students’ attitudes toward starting a business and their perceived behavioral control.

5.
Conclusion and Recommendations This study is based on the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) to explore the impact of the Fintech ecosystem on Vietnamese students’ intention to start a digital business, through the components of Fintech perception and environmental support.
For universities, this is a component that plays an important role in fostering entrepreneurial spirit. Therefore, universities can enhance Fintech knowledge, awareness, and skills by incorporating educational programs related to digital entrepreneurship and Fintech. Activities may include organizing entrepreneurship clubs, arranging educational programs that invite renowned entrepreneurs to bring the image of successful entrepreneurs closer to students, as well as practical skill activities and digital entrepreneurship competitions to encourage students to gain experience and access real-world practice early on. Universities can also organize extracurricular activities, digital entrepreneurship idea contests and competitions, as well as school-level and inter-university seminars to enhance students’ understanding of Fintech.
For the Government, digital entrepreneurship can reduce unemployment rates while simultaneously increasing GDP and fostering sustainable economic development. Therefore, the Government should establish a favorable ecosystem to encourage students' entrepreneurial spirit through institutions, legislation, and policies that promote a startup-friendly environment. In addition, the government should enhance awareness campaigns about Fintech and implement better support policies for businesses operating in the digital business sector.
This study still has certain limitations. First, it is cross-sectional in nature, whereas studying individual behavior requires a longitudinal approach to obtain more accurate results.
Therefore, future studies could adopt a longitudinal approach to examine the impact of Fintech perception on entrepreneurial behavior. Secondly, the Fintech ecosystem comprises numerous factors that influence the intention to start a digital business; however, this study only examined the effect of Fintech perception and environmental support. Thus, future research should consider a more comprehensive range of factors affecting the intention to start a digital business.

The solution set of the problem is denoted by 𝛺, that is, 𝛺 = {𝑥* ∈ 𝐶 : 𝐴𝑥* ∈ 𝑄}.
The split feasibility problem, first introduced by Censor and Elfving [1] in 1994, has numerous applications in signal processing, image processing, and so on (see, for example, [1-12]).
Specifically, consider a linear transformation as follows: 𝑦 = 𝐴𝑥, where 𝐴 is a sampling matrix of size 𝑀×𝑁, 𝑥 is the original signal vector of size 𝑁, and 𝑦 is the measured vector of size 𝑀. Typically, 𝑀 is much smaller than 𝑁, so how can we recover 𝑥 given 𝑦 and 𝐴? We know that in this case, there are 𝑀 equations for 𝑁 unknowns, but since 𝑀 is less than 𝑁, there is no unique solution. This scenario can be illustrated using the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT), in which 𝑦 consists of only 𝑀 frequencies out of 𝑁 total frequencies from the full Fourier transform. The question posed is whether it is possible to recover the signal 𝑥 from only the 𝑀 frequencies of the measured signal 𝑦. Interestingly, E. Candes, J. Romberg, and T. Tao, in [13], showed that the answer to this question is affirmative in the case where 𝑥 is a sparse signal, that is, 𝑥 has length 𝑁 but contains only 𝑙 nonzero components. Given a sparse signal 𝑥 of length 𝑁, with 𝑙 nonzero elements and the remaining 𝑁−𝑙 elements considered zero, the measurement vector 𝑦 is obtained through the projection using the sampling matrix 𝐴: 𝑦 = 𝐴𝑥 + 𝜂, where 𝜂 is Gaussian noise with zero mean and variance 𝜎2. To recover the original signal 𝑥̅, the problem reduces to solving the following minimization optimization problem: 𝑥̅ = argmin 2 ≤ 𝜀. (2) 𝑥 ||𝑥||1 subject to ||𝑦 − 𝐴𝑥||2 It is known that problem (2) is equivalent to the following problem: 𝑥̅ = argmin 2 subject to ||𝑥||1 ≤ 𝑡, (3) 𝑥 ||𝑦 − 𝐴𝑥||2 where 𝑡 is a suitable positive real number. If we set 𝐶 = {𝑥∈ℋ1: ||𝑥||1 ≤ 𝑡}, 𝑄 = {𝑦}, then solving problem (3) can be reduced to solving the SFP (1).
The split feasibility problem was initially studied by Censor and Elfving in finite-dimensional spaces, and was later investigated by many other mathematicians in infinite-dimensional spaces (see, for example, the articles and related references [2,5,6,8,9,11,12,14-19]). The most common iterative method for solving the SFP is the CQ algorithm proposed by Byrne in [3].
This method is a special case of the projected gradient algorithm for the convex optimization problem with optimal value 0: 2‖Ax - PQAx‖ As we know, the function 𝑓 is convex and differentiable, and ∇𝑓(𝑥) = A*(I - PQ)Ax, x ∈ ℋ₁ is strongly monotone inverse with constant 1/‖A‖², that is: ⟨∇𝑓(𝑥) - ∇𝑓(𝑦), x - y⟩ ≥ (1/‖A‖²)‖∇𝑓(𝑥) - ∇𝑓(𝑦)‖² for all x, y ∈ ℋ₁.
However, the CQ algorithm requires knowledge of the norm of the bounded linear operator, which is clearly not easy to compute.
Therefore, adaptive methods have been proposed; see, for example, [9,18,20,21]. Notably, López et al. in [9] developed a step size selection method such that when applying the CQ algorithm, information about the norm of the operator is not required.
It should be noted that the CQ method only achieves weak convergence in Hilbert spaces.
For other interesting results related to the split feasibility problem, see the survey by Ansari and Rehan [22] and recent results in [5,7,11,15–17,19].
In 2022, Huang and Liu [7] combined the multi-step gradient algorithm of Kesornprom, Pholasa, and Cholamjiak [8] with the conjugate gradient method of Sakurai and Iiduka [23] to propose a new conjugate gradient algorithm for solving the split feasibility problem and presented an application in the field of signal processing.
However, upon a thorough review of this paper, we have identified several drawbacks as follows: The computational workload remains quite large (requiring two evaluations of the function and its gradient); the convergence proof of the algorithm contains a gap after formula (19) (it can only be inferred that the iterative sequence is quasi-Fejér monotone, not Fejér monotone as stated in the paper); and assumption (C4) is insufficient to guarantee that the quantities 𝑀 and 𝑀̂ are finite.
Based on these analyses, in this paper we propose a CQ algorithm with a new conjugate gradient direction and prove its weak convergence. Our algorithm overcomes the limitations of Algorithm 1 in [7], while also providing a unified approach to CQ-type algorithms that use the Polyak step size as presented in recent studies, such as in [9,18,24]. CONCLUSION In this paper, we proposed an inertial CQ algorithm with conjugate gradient direction for solving the split feasibility problem (1) in Hilbert spaces. The step size is computed using the Polyak formula, which is highly effective when the optimal value is known. We showed that the sequence generated by the proposed algorithm converges weakly to a solution of the split feasibility problem.
Our algorithm has improved and extended several CQ methods with Polyak step size presented in recent studies. Numerical results demonstrate its effectiveness compared to known algorithms.

Results Dengue fever is an infectious disease caused by the Dengue virus.
This is one of 20 prominent infectious diseases that are regularly monitored, tracked, and reported through the weekly national epidemic surveillance system [1].
The Dengue virus includes four serotypes: DENV1, DENV2, DENV3, and DENV4. According to previous studies, the distribution of Dengue virus serotypes varies across different geographical regions, and the predominant serotype responsible for outbreaks alternates over time and by location [2]. The clinical manifestations caused by the different DENV serotypes are relatively similar [3]. However, the risk of developing severe Dengue hemorrhagic fever and secondary Dengue hemorrhagic fever differs among the DENV serotypes [4], [5]. In addition, there is evidence that antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE) contributes to more severe disease in individuals who experience reinfection with Dengue hemorrhagic fever [6]. Therefore, identifying the circulating serotypes plays an important role in supporting the prediction of epidemic trends, thereby providing early warning for the community to proactively prevent Dengue hemorrhagic fever.
In Hanoi, there have been many studies on severe Dengue hemorrhagic fever, but most have focused on the disease itself and the vector virus, with few investigating the distribution of serotypes over time, space, and among affected populations. This study aims to describe certain epidemiological characteristics of Dengue virus serotypes causing severe Dengue hemorrhagic fever in selected districts of Hanoi over a three-year period from 2017 to 2019.

Thus, 2017 coincided with this serotype’s activity cycle, which may have contributed to the large Dengue hemorrhagic fever outbreak in Vietnam. Several other studies have also confirmed that the 2017 outbreak was primarily caused by DENV1 [9], [10].
Among the three circulating serotypes identified at the study site, DENV1 accounted for the majority (60%), followed by DENV2 (38.5%); DENV4 accounted for a very small proportion. Virological surveillance results over nearly two decades have also recorded DENV-1 and DENV2 as the two predominant serotypes causing disease throughout Vietnam [8]. Our findings are consistent with the reports of Dang Thi Thuy and Takemura during the 2017 Dengue hemorrhagic fever outbreak, in which DENV1 played the primary pathogenic role [9], [10]. Other studies in Asia have also demonstrated that the distribution of circulating DENV serotypes varies by region. For example, a study by Cao et al. in China during 2014–2015 found that DENV1 predominated in Guangzhou, while DENV2 was predominant in Xishuangbanna [11].
The distribution of DENV serotypes in Japan, according to a study by Ommer and colleagues published in 2021, includes the circulation of three serotypes: DENV1, DENV2, and DENV3, with DENV2 playing the principal role in disease causation [12].
The differences among the four DENV serotypes in clinical symptoms are difficult to determine because their manifestations are relatively similar [3].
The results in Table 2 show that there is no difference in the rate of infection with DENV serotypes among the study groups, between children and adults, or between males and females.
A study in Saudi Arabia showed that the distribution of DENV serotypes did not differ between the two genders, while the distribution of DENV2 and DENV3 varied among age groups; however, the distribution of DENV1 was similar across age groups [12]. In this study, DENV1 accounted for the majority of cases, so the finding that there was no difference in the distribution of DENV serotypes among the study groups is consistent.
Our results are consistent with a study in Sri Lanka, which found that the distribution of DENV serotypes did not differ by age or gender [16]. The results in Table 4 show that there is no difference in DENV serotype circulation between rural and urban areas.
In Sri Lanka, the distribution of DENV1 and DENV3 varies between regions; however, there is no difference in the distribution of DENV2 and DENV4 [16].
In Cambodia, during the period 2012–2015, DENV4 accounted for only 9% and was mainly found in rural areas; in 2016 and 2017, DENV4 made up 70% and 67%, appearing in both rural areas and the capital city Vientiane (urban area) [17].
In Vietnam, there are still few studies analyzing the distribution of DENV serotypes by ecological region; therefore, further research is needed in other areas.
5.
Conclusion The study of the temporal distribution of DENV serotypes in Table 3 shows that the proportion of DENV1 serotype in the epidemic year—2017 (68.2%)—was higher than in non-epidemic years (45.4%). The higher prevalence of DENV1 in the epidemic year of 2017 may be due to a greater number of viruses present in the serum of individuals infected with DENV1 compared to other DENV serotypes [13]. This can in part be explained by the phenomenon of antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE), in which pre-existing antibodies from a primary Dengue infection bind to DENV particles during a subsequent infection with a different DENV serotype. The antibodies produced from the initial infection are not able to neutralize the virus.
Instead, the virus–antibody complex attaches to receptors known as Fcγ receptors on circulating mononuclear cells. The antibodies help the virus infect monocytes more efficiently.
The result is an overall increase in viral replication, leading to a higher viral load in the body [6].
Through the analysis of data from 270 patients with Dengue hemorrhagic fever whose causative serotype of Dengue fever was identified over three years (2017–2019) in Hanoi, we draw the following conclusions and recommendations: Each DENV serotype causing disease generally circulates in cycles [14], because after infection with a particular DENV serotype, the patient develops specific immunity to that serotype.
Therefore, other serotypes will take over. Three DENV serotypes causing disease were detected, with DENV1 having the highest proportion (60%), followed by DENV2 (38.5%), and DENV4 accounting for a very low rate (1.5%).
The distribution of DENV serotypes varied over time, with the proportion of DENV1 in the epidemic year of 2017 (68.2%) being higher than in the non-epidemic years of 2018 and 2019 (45.4%).

ABSTRACT Ganoderma lucidum is a valuable medicinal mushroom widely used in many countries due to its anticancer, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and immunomodulatory effects. Ganoderma lucidum contains a variety of bioactive metabolites, among which polysaccharides (PS) have been demonstrated to be one of the most prominent bioactive groups. The content and activity of these compounds depend on cultivation conditions, extraction methods, and species. This study was conducted to extract PS compounds and evaluate the biological activity of extracts from four types of Ganoderma lucidum mushrooms: Korean Ganoderma (LCH), cultivated Ganoderma (LCV), wild Ganoderma (LXR), and cultivated Lim green (LXV). The results showed that extraction with 80% ethanol yielded a higher PS content compared to water extraction, with the highest content in the LXV sample at 4.73 mg/g dry weight—126% higher than water extraction (3.73 mg/g). The lowest PS content was found in the LCH sample, with 3.42 mg/g by water extraction and 3.53 mg/g by ethanol extraction. Regarding antioxidant activity, the LXN extract exhibited the best result with an IC50 value of 51.35, while the lowest was in LCH with an IC50 of 59.04. Most extracts showed inhibitory effects against four tested bacterial strains, with the strongest activity comparable to streptomycin; the largest inhibition zone (8.0 mm against Bacillus) was recorded for the LXV extract, while the lowest efficacy was noted for the LCH sample, which only exhibited activity against Shigella sp.

Ganoderma lucidum is a medicinal mushroom that has been used as a valuable medicinal substance in the traditional medicine of several countries such as China, Korea, Japan, and Vietnam for centuries. Ganoderma lucidum is known to have numerous effects on human health, including anticancer, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory activities, blood pressure stabilization, regulation of metabolic disorders such as diabetes and obesity, and enhancement of immunity, among others. Studies on G. lucidum extracts have shown that their chemical composition includes 11.1% glucose, 10.2% minerals (including K, Mg, and Ca), and 7.3% protein. Notably, the important bioactive compounds include polysaccharides, triterpenes, flavonoids, alkaloids, steroids, unsaturated fatty acids, proteins, amino acids, enzymes, vitamins, and minerals. Among these, the therapeutic properties of G. lucidum are primarily attributed to its polysaccharides and triterpenoids, which are the focus of many studies [1].
